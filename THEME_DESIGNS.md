# Theme Progression Designs

Status: In review — first batch of 4 archetypes for pattern approval.

Design principles:
- Theme abilities complement class features, never replicate them
- Each tier should be useful both in and out of combat
- L1 = existing Background feature (kept as-is)
- L5 = small active or always-on ability that defines the Theme's edge
- L11 = Expertise Die (d4) on 2 signature skills + a supporting ability
- L17 = Capstone + Expertise Die scales to d6. Should feel legendary for the *concept*, not the combat role
- Similar Themes (Soldier vs Mercenary Veteran) must diverge meaningfully at L5+

---

## Soldier

**Identity:** Military discipline, leadership under pressure, tactical awareness. The Soldier isn't just someone who fights — a Fighter does that. The Soldier is someone who was *trained by an institution*, who understands chain of command, unit cohesion, and what it means to hold a line when everything goes wrong.

**Signature Skills:** Athletics, Intimidation

**How it diverges from Mercenary Veteran:** Soldier is about *duty and discipline* — institutional loyalty, leadership, protecting others. Mercenary Veteran is about *pragmatism and survival* — no loyalty except to the contract, reading a battlefield for personal advantage.

### L1 — Military Rank (existing)
You have a military rank from your career as a soldier. Soldiers loyal to your former military organization still recognize your authority and influence, and they defer to you if they are of a lower rank. You can invoke your rank to exert influence over other soldiers and requisition simple equipment or horses for temporary use from allied military outposts.

### L5 — Field Discipline
Your military training has conditioned your body and mind for the demands of campaign life.
- You require only 4 hours of sleep to gain the benefits of a long rest (or 4 hours of trance if you are an elf). The remaining time must be spent in light activity.
- During a short rest, you can tend to your weapons and armor, granting yourself **+1 AC** until the end of your next combat encounter (doesn't stack with itself).
- You have **advantage on Constitution saving throws** against exhaustion from forced marches, harsh weather, and sleep deprivation.

*Reasoning: This is the "campaigner" ability — the Soldier is harder to wear down than anyone else. The +1 AC from maintenance rewards short rests without being a combat power. The reduced sleep lets them take watches others can't.*

### L11 — Rally
**Expertise Die (d4)** on Athletics and Intimidation checks.

Once per long rest, as a **bonus action**, you rally your allies. Up to **3 allies within 30 feet** who can hear you gain **temporary hit points** equal to your level + your Charisma modifier (minimum 1).

*Reasoning: The Soldier's L11 identity is "leader." Rally is simple, impactful, and works regardless of class. A Soldier Wizard rallying the party is just as evocative as a Soldier Fighter doing it. Temporary HP is clean — no stacking issues, no concentration, just a morale boost.*

### L17 — Unbreakable
**Expertise Die scales to d6** on Athletics and Intimidation.

- You have **advantage on saving throws** against being frightened, charmed, and stunned.
- Once per long rest, when you or an **ally within 30 feet** would fail a death saving throw, you can call out to them — they succeed instead. ("Not today, soldier.")
- When you are reduced to 0 hit points but not killed outright, you can use your reaction to make one weapon attack or cast one cantrip before falling unconscious. Once per long rest.

*Reasoning: The Soldier's capstone is about being the last one standing and refusing to let anyone die on their watch. The death save intervention is powerful but limited (once/day). The "one last strike" is cinematic — the old soldier who takes the hit and still swings back.*

## Sage

**Identity:** Scholar, researcher, keeper of accumulated knowledge. The Sage isn't just smart — an INT-based class handles that. The Sage is someone who has *studied systematically*, who knows how to find answers, cross-reference sources, and apply obscure knowledge at the critical moment.

**Signature Skills:** Arcana, History

**How it diverges from Hermit:** Sage is about *institutional knowledge* — libraries, universities, mentors, formal study. Hermit is about *personal revelation* — isolation, meditation, discovering truths the institutions missed.

### L1 — Researcher (existing)
When you attempt to learn or recall a piece of lore, if you do not know that information, you often know where and from whom you can obtain it. Usually, this information comes from a library, scriptorium, university, or a sage or other learned person or creature.

### L5 — Studied Mind
Your years of study have given you an unusually comprehensive mind.
- During a **short rest** spent studying a text, you can **read and comprehend any written language** (even ones you don't speak). You understand the meaning but do not gain the ability to speak or write the language. Complex or lengthy texts may require multiple short rests.
- Once per long rest, you can **recall a relevant piece of lore** — ask the DM one question about the history, nature, or properties of a creature, object, location, or event. The DM provides truthful, though potentially incomplete, information.

*Reasoning: The Sage's edge is "I always know something useful." Comprehend Languages during a short rest feels earned — you're actually sitting down and puzzling through the grammar, not glancing at it for 10 minutes. The lore recall is a once/day "ask the DM a question" that rewards curiosity and gives the AI DM a hook to deliver world-building.*

### L11 — Analytical Eye
**Expertise Die (d4)** on Arcana and History checks.

When you **observe a creature for at least 1 minute** (or succeed on a **DC 15 Intelligence check as an action** in combat), you learn:
- Its **damage vulnerabilities, resistances, and immunities**
- Whether it is **under any magical effects**
- Its **creature type** and approximate **challenge rating** (weak/moderate/dangerous/deadly relative to your level)

*Reasoning: The Sage's L11 identity is "analyst." Knowing enemy resistances and vulnerabilities is powerful tactical information that helps the whole party, but it requires investment (a full action in combat, or a minute of observation). This makes the Sage the person everyone looks to before a fight — "What do you know about these things?"*

### L17 — Living Library
**Expertise Die scales to d6** on Arcana and History.

- You have **advantage on all Intelligence checks.**
- Once per long rest, you can **study a creature, object, or location** for 10 minutes and learn everything that can be known about it through non-magical investigation — its origins, its purpose, its weaknesses, its history, and any connections to other known subjects. The DM reveals comprehensive information, including details that would normally require specialized knowledge or access to rare sources. This is the Sage's mind drawing connections across a lifetime of reading.
- When you or an ally within 30 feet makes an Intelligence, Wisdom, or Charisma check related to **lore, history, or arcane knowledge**, you can use your **reaction** to grant them your Expertise Die on the roll.

*Reasoning: The Sage's capstone is "the wisest person in the room." Advantage on all INT checks is clean and broadly useful. The study ability is framed as active analysis of something present (creature, object, location) rather than recall of something past — the Sage examines the ancient door and deduces its origin, purpose, and how to open it. This doesn't rely on the DM remembering prior sessions. Sharing the Expertise Die lets the Sage help others on knowledge checks, reinforcing their role as the party's brain.*

## Criminal

**Identity:** Someone who operates outside the law — not necessarily evil, but someone who understands how the underworld works, how to get what you need through channels most people don't know exist, and how to disappear when things go wrong.

**Signature Skills:** Stealth, Deception

**How it diverges from Charlatan:** Criminal is about *operating in the shadows* — breaking in, escaping, knowing the underworld. Charlatan is about *social manipulation* — wearing masks, running cons, becoming someone else. Criminal's edge is physical (stealth, locks, escape). Charlatan's edge is social (disguise, persuasion, identity).

### L1 — Criminal Contact (existing)
You have a reliable and trustworthy contact who acts as your liaison to a network of other criminals. You know how to get messages to and from your contact, even over great distances; specifically, you know the local messengers, corrupt caravan masters, and seedy sailors who can deliver messages for you.

### L5 — Case the Joint
Your criminal experience lets you read a location's vulnerabilities at a glance.
- After spending **10 minutes observing** a building, camp, or enclosed area, you learn the **number of exits**, **approximate number of occupants**, and you **automatically detect traps and alarms with a DC of 15 or lower**. For traps and alarms above DC 15, you have **advantage on the Perception or Investigation check** to detect them.
- You have **advantage on Dexterity checks** to pick locks and disarm non-magical traps.
- You can communicate silently with allies using **thieves' cant gestures** visible up to 60 feet (even if your class doesn't grant thieves' cant).

*Reasoning: Case the Joint makes the Criminal the person who plans the infiltration. It's useful before any heist, prison break, or stealth mission, but it requires 10 minutes — you can't use it reactively. The DC 15 threshold defines "obvious" clearly — simple tripwires and basic alarms are auto-detected, but sophisticated traps still require a check (with advantage). The lock/trap advantage is the Criminal's bread and butter. Thieves' cant gestures are a nice flavor touch that enables silent coordination.*

### L11 — Underworld Network
**Expertise Die (d4)** on Stealth and Deception checks.

Your criminal contacts span multiple cities. In any settlement of **town size or larger**, you can find a local contact within **1d4 hours** who can provide:
- Local **rumors and intelligence** (who's in power, what's being smuggled, where the guards don't patrol)
- **Black market goods** at 150% of market price (including items that aren't sold openly)
- A **safe house** for up to 3 days (no questions asked)

Once per long rest, you can **call in a favor** — your network will deliver a message, acquire a specific non-magical item, create a distraction in a public place, or provide an alibi.

*Reasoning: The Criminal's L11 identity is "connected." This is the infrastructure version of Criminal Contact — it scales from one contact to a web. The "call in a favor" is powerful but limited. It creates interesting narrative moments: "I know a guy in this city."*

### L17 — Ghost
**Expertise Die scales to d6** on Stealth and Deception.

- Creatures attempting to track you have **disadvantage on Survival checks**, and the DC to track you increases by **5**. You can still be tracked — it's just very, very hard.
- You have **advantage on initiative rolls** when you are hidden at the start of combat.
- Once per long rest, as an action, you can **vanish from plain sight**. You become invisible until the **end of your next turn** (no concentration required). The **first attack or spell you make** while invisible doesn't break the invisibility — after that first attack or spell resolves, the invisibility ends. After it ends, you leave no trace of your presence in the area — no footprints, no scent, no residual magic.

*Reasoning: The Criminal's capstone is "nearly untouchable." Tracking is extremely hard but not impossible — the fun of being hunted is preserved, it's just that you're really good at running. Advantage on initiative from stealth rewards the Criminal's playstyle without replicating Alert. The vanish gives exactly one guaranteed strike from invisibility (advantage on the attack, auto-crit if within 5 feet for Rogues), then you're visible. Strong but tightly scoped — once per day, one free shot, then you'd better have an exit plan.*

## Acolyte

**Identity:** Temple-trained servant of a deity or faith. The Acolyte isn't just a believer — a Cleric or Paladin handles divine power. The Acolyte is someone who was *raised in a temple*, who understands ritual, community, and the quiet strength of faith. Their power comes from devotion and spiritual practice, not from divine spellcasting.

**Signature Skills:** Insight, Religion

**How it diverges from Hermit:** Acolyte is about *communal faith* — temples, congregations, shared ritual, service to others. Hermit is about *solitary truth* — withdrawal from society, personal revelation, inner knowledge that others haven't found.

### L1 — Shelter of the Faithful (existing)
As an acolyte, you command the respect of those who share your faith, and you can perform the religious ceremonies of your deity. You and your adventuring companions can receive free healing and care at a temple, shrine, or other established presence of your faith, though you must provide any material components needed for spells. Those who share your religion will support you (but only you) at a modest lifestyle.

### L5 — Spiritual Counsel
Your years of temple service have taught you to tend to the spirit as well as the body.
- Once per long rest, you can spend **10 minutes in prayer or meditation** with a willing creature. That creature gains **advantage on the next Wisdom saving throw** it makes within the next 24 hours.
- When you spend at least **10 minutes in conversation** with a creature, you can determine their **emotional state**, whether they are **hiding something** (though not what), and their **general disposition** toward you and your party. This is not magic — it's years of pastoral training in reading people.
- When you provide care to a creature during a **short rest**, they regain one additional **hit die** worth of healing.

*Reasoning: Spiritual Counsel makes the Acolyte the emotional anchor of the party. The Wisdom save advantage is a preemptive ward — the Acolyte prays with you before you enter the haunted ruin. The pastoral read replaces the niche consecration sense — it's always useful and fits the "temple counselor" identity. The short rest healing is the Acolyte's "I tend to your wounds and your spirit" — small but consistent value.*

### L11 — Faithkeeper
**Expertise Die (d4)** on Insight and Religion checks.

Once per long rest, you can perform a **1-minute blessing ceremony**. Up to **6 creatures** who participate gain the benefit of the **Bless spell** (+1d4 to attack rolls and saving throws) for **1 hour**. This is not a spell — it cannot be counterspelled, dispelled, or ended by an antimagic field. It does not require concentration.

*Reasoning: The Acolyte's L11 identity is "spiritual leader." A 1-hour non-concentration, non-spell Bless is genuinely powerful — but it requires a full minute of ceremony (can't use it in combat), affects the whole group, and is once per day. This is the Acolyte gathering the party before a dangerous day and performing a ritual that actually matters mechanically. It also works beautifully with Synergies — the Soldier rallying + the Acolyte blessing before a siege.*

### L17 — Divine Intercessor
**Expertise Die scales to d6** on Insight and Religion.

Your lifelong devotion has earned you a direct line to your deity's attention. You gain the following:
- **Aura of Conviction:** You and all allies within **10 feet** have **advantage on saving throws against being frightened, charmed, and possessed**. This aura is always active and is not a spell.
- Once per long rest, you can beseech your deity for **direct intervention**. Choose one:
  - **Heal:** A creature within 60 feet is healed for **4d10 + your Wisdom modifier** hit points and is cured of one disease or one condition (blinded, deafened, paralyzed, or poisoned).
  - **Revive:** A creature that died within the last minute is returned to life with 1 HP (as Revivify, but requires **no material components** — just your prayer).
  - **Commune:** You receive the answer to a single question as if you had cast the Commune spell. The answer goes beyond yes/no — your deity provides a brief vision or message with context.
  - **Sanctuary:** You create a 30-foot radius zone of sacred ground for 10 minutes. Undead and fiends cannot willingly enter the zone. Creatures resting within the zone recover **maximum hit die rolls** instead of rolling.

All intervention effects are **divine, not magical** — they cannot be counterspelled, dispelled, or suppressed by antimagic fields.

*Reasoning: The Acolyte's capstone is "your faith has weight." The passive aura gives the Acolyte a persistent presence — you always feel safer with them nearby, and it stacks meaningfully with the L11 Faithkeeper blessing. The intervention options give flexibility for what the party needs. The Revivify without components is the headline (300gp diamond saved). The enhanced Commune goes beyond yes/no to match the "Living Library" study ability from Sage — both capstones unlock deep information, just through different means. The Sanctuary's max-HP-on-rest makes the Acolyte the person you want watching over camp. Everything is framed as prayer answered, not magic cast.*

## Design Pattern Summary

| Tier | Purpose | Power Level | Combat/Non-Combat |
|------|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| L1 | Existing Background feature | Ribbon / utility | Mostly non-combat |
| L5 | Defines the Theme's edge | Minor but always-on | Both — leans non-combat |
| L11 | Expertise Die + signature ability | Moderate, once/day or limited | Both — the "do your thing" moment |
| L17 | Capstone + d6 scaling | Strong, once/day, multiple options | Both — the "legendary" moment |

Each Theme should hit these beats:
1. **L5 answers:** "What can I do that nobody else in the party can?" (Case the Joint, Studied Mind, Spiritual Counsel, Field Discipline)
2. **L11 answers:** "What am I the *best* at, and how does that help the group?" (Underworld Network, Analytical Eye, Faithkeeper, Rally)
3. **L17 answers:** "What makes me legendary in my domain?" (Ghost, Living Library, Divine Intercessor, Unbreakable)

## Charlatan

**Identity:** A master of false identities, cons, and social manipulation. The Charlatan doesn't fight the world head-on — they make the world believe whatever is most convenient. Their power is in becoming someone else so convincingly that reality bends around the lie.

**Signature Skills:** Deception, Sleight of Hand

**How it diverges from Criminal:** Criminal operates in the shadows — breaking in, escaping, knowing the underworld. Charlatan operates in plain sight — the con happens while you're looking right at them. Criminal's edge is physical (stealth, locks, escape). Charlatan's edge is social (identity, manipulation, misdirection).

### L1 — False Identity (existing)
You have created a second identity that includes documentation, established acquaintances, and disguises that allow you to assume that persona. Additionally, you can forge documents including official papers and personal letters, as long as you have seen an example of the kind of document or the handwriting you are trying to copy.

### L5 — Second Skin
Your talent for deception has become instinctive.
- You can create a **convincing disguise** in **1 minute** instead of the usual 10 minutes (using a disguise kit or improvised materials). Creatures have **disadvantage on Insight checks** to see through your disguises.
- You can **mimic the voice and speech patterns** of any person you've heard speak for at least 10 minutes. A suspicious listener can make a Wisdom (Insight) check contested by your Charisma (Deception) to detect the mimicry.
- You have **advantage on Charisma checks** made while operating under a false identity that you've maintained for at least 24 hours.

*Reasoning: The Charlatan's L5 is "I become other people." Fast disguise with disadvantage to pierce it makes the Charlatan the master of infiltration-by-identity rather than infiltration-by-stealth. Voice mimicry opens up distraction and misdirection tactics. The 24-hour identity advantage rewards commitment to a cover story.*

### L11 — Web of Lies
**Expertise Die (d4)** on Deception and Sleight of Hand checks.

Your network of false identities has become an infrastructure.
- You can maintain up to **3 false identities** simultaneously (each with their own documentation, reputation, and contacts). Switching between identities requires 10 minutes and access to your disguise kit.
- Once per long rest, you can **plant a false memory or belief** in a creature you spend at least 1 minute in conversation with. The target makes a **Wisdom saving throw (DC = 8 + your proficiency bonus + your Charisma modifier)**. On a failure, they believe one false statement you told them for 24 hours, unless presented with undeniable evidence to the contrary. This is not magic — it's pure manipulation.
- You can **read a mark** — after observing a creature for 1 minute, you learn **one** of the following (your choice): their **greatest desire**, their **biggest insecurity**, or **what they're most afraid of losing**. You can only read a given creature once per long rest — first impressions are the Charlatan's sharpest tool.

*Reasoning: The Charlatan's L11 identity is "puppeteer." Three maintained identities is an infrastructure tool — the Charlatan has a merchant persona, a noble persona, and a guard persona ready to go. The false belief planting is the Charlatan's signature power — social mind control without magic, limited by a save and 24-hour duration. Reading a mark gives them the information to run a con on anyone.*

### L17 — The Perfect Con
**Expertise Die scales to d6** on Deception and Sleight of Hand.

- You can **attempt to lie even under Zone of Truth** and similar compulsion magic — you are not automatically compelled to speak truth. However, you must succeed on a **Charisma (Deception) check contested by the caster's spell save DC** to make the lie believable. On a failure, the magic holds and you must speak truthfully. You are always aware when such magic is being used on you.
- Once per long rest, you can assume a **flawless identity** for up to 8 hours. For the duration, you radiate the appropriate aura for the identity (appearing as the correct alignment, creature type, and class to divination magic), your voice and mannerisms are indistinguishable from the real person (if impersonating someone specific), and even magical means of detection (Detect Thoughts, Zone of Truth, True Seeing) must succeed on a **contested check against your Deception** to penetrate the disguise. This is not a spell — it's the apex of mortal deception.
- When your **false belief** (from L11) would expire, you can spend 1 minute reinforcing it. The target must make the saving throw again to break free, and the DC increases by 2 for each reinforcement (max +6).

*Reasoning: The Charlatan's capstone is "the lie is real." Immunity to Zone of Truth is the headline — the ultimate expression of "you can't make me tell the truth." The flawless identity is the Perfect Con — for 8 hours, you ARE the queen, the archmage, the general, and even magic struggles to say otherwise. The reinforced false belief lets a long con last weeks or months with regular maintenance.*

## Entertainer

**Identity:** A performer whose art moves people — literally. The Entertainer isn't just charming (a Bard handles that mechanically). The Entertainer is someone who understands *audiences*, who can read a room, command attention, and turn a crowd's mood on a word. Their power is in connection and spectacle.

**Signature Skills:** Performance, Acrobatics

**How it diverges from Charlatan:** Charlatan deceives individuals through false identity. Entertainer moves crowds through genuine (or at least spectacular) performance. Charlatan works best one-on-one in private. Entertainer works best one-to-many in public. Charlatan's tools are lies. Entertainer's tools are truth wrapped in spectacle.

### L1 — By Popular Demand (existing)
You can always find a place to perform, usually in an inn or tavern but possibly with a circus, at a theater, or even in a noble's court. At such a place, you receive free lodging and food of a modest or comfortable standard, as long as you perform each night. In addition, your performance makes you something of a local figure. When strangers recognize you in a town where you have performed, they typically take a friendly attitude toward you.

### L5 — Read the Room
Your performer's instincts let you sense and manipulate the emotional temperature of any group.
- When you enter a room, building, or public space with **5 or more people**, you immediately sense the **prevailing mood** (hostile, fearful, celebratory, suspicious, bored, etc.) and can identify the **most influential person present** (the leader, the agitator, or the person everyone is watching).
- Once per short rest, you can **shift the mood** of a crowd of up to **30 people** by performing for at least 1 minute. Make a **Charisma (Performance) check** — the DC depends on how far you're pushing: calming a nervous crowd (DC 10), exciting a bored one (DC 13), turning a hostile crowd sympathetic (DC 18), or inciting a peaceful crowd to action (DC 20). On success, the crowd's general behavior shifts accordingly. On failure by 5 or more, the crowd turns against you. Individual important NPCs can resist with a Wisdom save.
- You have **advantage on Charisma checks** when you have an audience of 3 or more people watching.

*Reasoning: Read the Room makes the Entertainer the party's crowd control specialist — not through magic, but through presence and performance. Sensing mood and influence is passive intelligence gathering that's always useful in social situations. Mood shifting uses a Performance check with scaled DCs rather than a behind-the-scenes mood counter — the DM narrates the crowd's response based on the check result. The audience advantage means the Entertainer thrives in public.*

### L11 — Showstopper
**Expertise Die (d4)** on Performance and Acrobatics checks.

- Once per long rest, you can deliver a **performance so captivating** that every creature within 60 feet that can see and hear you must make a **Wisdom saving throw (DC = 8 + proficiency + Charisma modifier)** or be **charmed** by you for 1 minute. A charmed creature regards you as a friendly acquaintance and watches your performance with rapt attention. If you or your allies do anything harmful, the effect ends. This is not a spell — it's pure artistry.
- **Fame is a double-edged sword.** In any settlement where you've performed before (or where travelers from such a place might be):
  - You have **advantage on Persuasion checks** to request favors, gain audiences, or acquire information. People want to help the famous performer.
  - Admirers occasionally offer **gifts** — small tokens, free drinks, a family heirloom, a useful piece of local gossip. The DM determines the nature and frequency, but you can expect at least one gift per settlement visit.
  - However, you have **disadvantage on Stealth and Deception checks** to go unrecognized in places where your fame has spread. You are a known face — hiding in a crowd or pretending to be someone else is harder when people are looking for you.

*Reasoning: The Entertainer's L11 is "star power" — and star power cuts both ways. The captivating performance is a mass charm that can freeze a room. The reputation network opens doors and brings gifts, but being recognizable means the Entertainer can't easily disappear. This creates a real gameplay tension: fame helps socially but hurts stealth. It pairs interestingly with the Criminal Theme in a party — the Criminal does the sneaking, the Entertainer does the talking.*

### L17 — Legend of the Stage
**Expertise Die scales to d6** on Performance and Acrobatics.

- Your voice, instrument, or art carries **supernatural resonance** (this is not magic — it's the peak of mortal artistry reaching something transcendent). Once per long rest, choose one:
  - **Anthem of Valor:** You perform for 1 minute. All allies who can hear you gain **advantage on attack rolls and saving throws** for the next 10 minutes. This cannot be dispelled.
  - **Dirge of Sorrow:** You perform for 1 minute. All enemies within 120 feet who can hear you must make a Wisdom save or be **frightened** of you for 1 minute and have **disadvantage on their next attack roll**. Creatures that succeed are immune for 24 hours.
  - **Ballad of Mending:** You perform for 10 minutes. All allies who can hear you regain hit points equal to **twice their level** and are cured of one condition (poisoned, frightened, charmed, or exhaustion level 1).
- Your fame has become **legendary**. Your name is known across the realm.
  - You have **advantage on all Charisma checks** with creatures who have heard of you (which, at this level, is most people in civilized areas).
  - Admirers may offer **significant gifts** — enchanted instruments, invitations to perform at royal courts, or valuable favors from powerful fans. Once per campaign arc, a fan in a position of power offers meaningful aid (shelter, information, political cover) purely because of your legend.
  - The **stealth/recognition penalty intensifies**: you have **disadvantage on all Stealth and Deception checks** to go unrecognized in any civilized area. At this level of fame, you are recognized even in cities you've never visited. Disguises require a DC 15 Deception check just to avoid being spotted by a keen-eyed fan.

*Reasoning: The Entertainer's capstone is "your art changes the world — and the world knows your face." Three performance options mirror the Acolyte's intervention choices — adapt to what the moment needs. Anthem of Valor is a pre-battle rallying cry. Dirge of Sorrow is battlefield crowd control through sheer emotional power. Ballad of Mending is post-battle recovery. Legendary fame opens the biggest doors and brings the best gifts, but the recognition penalty is now severe — the Entertainer cannot hide. This is a feature, not a bug: it creates dramatic tension and forces creative problem-solving when discretion is needed.*

## Noble

**Identity:** Born to privilege, trained in politics, and accustomed to power. The Noble isn't just rich — wealth is a game mechanic anyone can acquire. The Noble understands *authority*, how to wield it, how to navigate the people who have it, and how to make others believe you deserve it even when you're far from court.

**Signature Skills:** Persuasion, History

**How it diverges from Knight of the Order:** Noble's power comes from *birthright and political acumen* — name, family connections, social rank. Knight of the Order's power comes from *sworn service and institutional authority* — they represent an organization. A Noble commands respect because of who they are. A Knight commands respect because of what they serve.

### L1 — Position of Privilege (existing)
Thanks to your noble birth, people are inclined to think the best of you. You are welcome in high society, and people assume you have the right to be wherever you are. The common folk make every effort to accommodate you and avoid your displeasure, and other people of high birth treat you as a member of the same social sphere. You can secure an audience with a local noble if you need to.

### L5 — Weight of Name
Your noble training gives you an instinctive command of social hierarchies and political maneuvering.
- You can **identify the political structure** of any settlement after spending **1 hour** observing or asking questions — who holds power, who is rivals with whom, who is rising and who is falling, and where the real influence lies (which may differ from the official structure).
- Once per long rest, you can **invoke your family name or noble bearing** to gain an immediate audience with the highest-ranking official available in a settlement. They are not obligated to help you, but they will hear you out.
- You have **advantage on Insight checks** to detect political motives, hidden alliances, and power plays in social situations.

*Reasoning: The Noble's L5 is "I understand power." Political structure mapping is a scouting tool for social environments — the Noble walks into a new city and quickly knows who actually runs things. The audience guarantee ensures access. The Insight advantage makes the Noble the person who spots the lie at the royal banquet.*

### L11 — Noblesse Oblige
**Expertise Die (d4)** on Persuasion and History checks.

- You can **command authority** over commoners and low-ranking officials. Once per long rest, you can issue a **command to a group of up to 10 non-hostile humanoids** of a social rank below yours. They follow a single reasonable instruction (clear a road, provide supplies, deliver a message, grant passage) unless it would put them in obvious danger. This is not magic — it's the weight of centuries of social conditioning. A Wisdom save (DC = 8 + proficiency + Charisma modifier) resists, but most common folk don't think to resist a noble's command.
- You have access to **noble hospitality**. In any settlement with a noble house, you can secure **comfortable lodging, meals, and basic services** for your entire party for up to 3 days by presenting yourself as a visiting dignitary. You are expected to reciprocate within **30 in-game days** — a return favor, a gift of equivalent value (50+ gp), or a service rendered to the host house. If you fail to reciprocate, your reputation suffers: you have **disadvantage on Persuasion checks** with nobility in that region until the debt is repaid, and word of your poor manners spreads to neighboring noble houses within 1d4 weeks.
- Once per long rest, you can spend 10 minutes **writing a letter of introduction** for yourself or an ally. The bearer of the letter has **advantage on their first Charisma check** with the recipient.

*Reasoning: The Noble's L11 is "authority that opens doors." The command ability is the Noble's version of the Criminal's "call in a favor" — both leverage networks, but the Noble's works through social rank rather than underworld connections. Noble hospitality is a party-wide lifestyle upgrade. Letters of introduction are a nice social utility that extends the Noble's influence to allies.*

### L17 — Sovereign Presence
**Expertise Die scales to d6** on Persuasion and History.

- You project an **aura of authority** that is almost tangible. Creatures meeting you for the first time must make a **Wisdom save (DC = 8 + proficiency + Charisma modifier)** or regard you as a person of great importance and treat you with deference for the next hour, regardless of your actual station. Hostile creatures are not affected, but neutral and indifferent creatures are.
- Once per long rest, you can **broker a negotiation** between two opposing parties (factions, nations, individuals). Both sides regard you as a credible neutral party and will agree to at least **hear each other's terms** under your mediation. You have advantage on all Persuasion and Insight checks for the duration of the negotiation.
- You can **call in a major political favor** once per campaign arc (not per rest — this is a story-level ability). A noble house, a city official, or a political faction owes you something significant and will provide material aid — soldiers, funding, political cover, or access to restricted resources. The DM determines the scope based on your history and standing.

*Reasoning: The Noble's capstone is "kingmaker." The aura of authority is passive social dominance — you walk in and people assume you matter. Brokering negotiations gives the Noble a unique conflict-resolution ability that no other Theme or class provides — the Noble can literally sit two warring factions at a table. The campaign-level political favor is the Noble's "Divine Intercessor" equivalent — a once-per-arc ability that changes the shape of the story. It's deliberately scoped to campaign arcs, not rests, because political capital is narrative, not renewable.*

## Outlander

**Identity:** Raised in the wild, far from cities and civilization. The Outlander isn't just someone who can survive outdoors — a Ranger handles wilderness combat. The Outlander is someone for whom the wilderness *is* home. They read the land the way a Noble reads a court, and they are most comfortable where most people are most afraid.

**Signature Skills:** Survival, Athletics

**Biome specialization (chosen at character creation):** The Outlander selects one biome as their home terrain: **forest, desert, mountain, arctic, swamp, coastal, grasslands, underground, or extra-planar**. All biome-specific abilities throughout the Theme progression apply only to this chosen biome. An Outlander of the desert reads sand and sun; an Outlander of the underground reads stone and echo. They are masters of *one* wilderness, not all.

**How it diverges from Far Traveler:** Outlander is rooted — they know *one* wilderness deeply and intimately. Far Traveler is broad — they've crossed many lands and cultures and carry wisdom from all of them. Outlander's edge is depth of knowledge about the natural world. Far Traveler's edge is breadth of experience across many worlds.

### L1 — Wanderer (existing)
You have an excellent memory for maps and geography, and you can always recall the general layout of terrain, settlements, and other features around you. In addition, you can find food and fresh water for yourself and up to five other people each day, provided that the land offers berries, small game, water, and so forth.

### L5 — Voice of the Wild
The wilderness speaks to you in ways others can't perceive.
- You can **predict the weather** accurately for the next **48 hours** by spending 10 minutes reading natural signs (cloud patterns, animal behavior, wind shifts). This extends to severe weather and natural disasters — you know a storm is coming hours before it arrives.
- You can **identify any natural plant, animal, or mineral** on sight, including whether it is safe to eat, poisonous, medicinal, or otherwise useful. No check required for common species; **DC 15 Nature check** for rare or magical specimens.
- When traveling through wilderness, your party's **travel pace is not slowed** by difficult terrain, and you cannot become lost by nonmagical means.

*Reasoning: The Outlander's L5 is "the wild is an open book." Weather prediction pairs perfectly with the existing weather system. Plant/animal identification feeds into the survival and crafting systems. Ignoring difficult terrain during travel is the Outlander earning their keep on every overland journey — the party moves faster with them.*

### L11 — Apex Predator
**Expertise Die (d4)** on Survival and Athletics checks.

- You can **track any creature** across any terrain, even through water, over stone, or after rain. You automatically succeed on Survival checks to track creatures with a DC of 15 or lower. For higher DCs, you have advantage.
- Once per long rest, when you are in a natural environment (not urban), you can **commune with the land** for 10 minutes. You learn the locations of any **large creatures, water sources, shelter, and settlements** within a **5-mile radius**. You also sense whether the land has been **disturbed by unnatural forces** (undead, aberrations, fiends, large-scale magic).
- **Natural camouflage:** When you are in **your chosen biome** and not wearing heavy armor, you can attempt to **hide even when only lightly obscured** by natural phenomena (foliage, rain, mist, snow, dim light). The Stealth check DC to remain hidden is **reduced by 5** compared to normal (DM sets the base DC as usual; your biome mastery gives you the edge).

*Reasoning: The Outlander's L11 is "master of the territory." The tracking upgrade makes them the definitive tracker — where the Criminal is untraceable, the Outlander is the one who finds them. Land communion is a 5-mile radius recon tool that replaces hours of scouting. Natural camouflage gives them a stealth edge in their home biome without stepping on the Criminal's urban stealth — the DC reduction is quantified so the DM knows exactly how much easier it is.*

### L17 — One with the Land
**Expertise Die scales to d6** on Survival and Athletics.

- You have **resistance to environmental damage** native to your **chosen biome** (cold damage in arctic, fire damage in desert, poison damage in swamp, psychic damage in extra-planar, etc.). You are **immune to the natural hazards** of your chosen biome (extreme temperatures, altitude sickness, quicksand, toxic spores, etc.).
- Once per long rest, you can enter a state of **primal awareness** for 1 hour. During this time:
  - You have **blindsight out to 30 feet** (you sense vibrations, air currents, and heat)
  - You have **advantage on all Wisdom checks and saving throws**
  - You can communicate basic concepts (danger, food, shelter, direction) with **any beast or plant**, and they are not hostile toward you unless magically compelled
- When you take a **long rest in your chosen biome**, you and up to 6 allies regain **maximum hit die rolls** instead of rolling, and each ally **recovers one additional hit die**. The land restores those who know how to rest within it.

*Reasoning: The Outlander's capstone is "the wilderness IS you." Biome-specific environmental resistance and immunity make them unkillable by their home terrain. Primal awareness is a sensory upgrade that makes them supernaturally attuned — blindsight from reading the environment, beast communication, heightened wisdom. The biome-locked long rest bonus mirrors the Acolyte's Sanctuary (max hit die), but the Outlander provides it only in their chosen biome — it's incredibly powerful there, but useless in a dungeon if you're a forest Outlander.*

## Sailor

**Identity:** A life lived on the water — not just someone who can row a boat, but someone shaped by the sea's rhythms, its dangers, and its community. The Sailor understands currents, weather, navigation, and the particular loyalty that forms between people who depend on each other to survive.

**Signature Skills:** Perception, Athletics

**How it diverges from Outlander:** Outlander is about *land wilderness* — forests, mountains, tundra. Sailor is about *the sea and waterways* — navigation, storms, ships, coastal settlements. Outlander reads terrain. Sailor reads water, wind, and the people who live by them. Their social skills are different too — the Outlander is solitary, the Sailor is communal (a ship requires a crew).

### L1 — Ship's Passage (existing)
When you need to, you can secure free passage on a sailing ship for yourself and your adventuring companions. You might sail on the ship you served on, or another ship you have good relations with. Because you're calling in a favor, you can't be certain of a schedule or route that will meet your every need. Your DM will determine how long it takes to get where you need to go. In return for your free passage, you and your companions are expected to assist the crew during the voyage.

### L5 — Sea Legs
The sea has conditioned you for a life of constant motion and harsh conditions.
- You have a **swimming speed equal to your walking speed**, and you can **hold your breath** for a number of minutes equal to **twice your Constitution modifier** (minimum 2 minutes).
- You have **advantage on Dexterity saving throws** against effects that would knock you prone, and you cannot be knocked prone by **wave, wind, or vehicle movement** (you keep your footing on a rocking deck instinctively).
- In any **port town or coastal settlement**, you can find a trustworthy contact within **1d4 hours** — a dockworker, a fisherman, a smuggler, or a harbormaster — who can provide local information, rumors, or passage.

*Reasoning: The Sailor's L5 is "the sea made me." Swimming speed and breath holding are the practical payoffs. Sea weather prediction mirrors the Outlander's land weather but for maritime contexts. The prone resistance is the "sea legs" flavor — you don't fall down. The port contact is the social angle — Sailors know people in every port, like the Criminal knows people in every city.*

### L11 — Old Salt
**Expertise Die (d4)** on Perception and Athletics checks.

- You can **navigate by the stars** without any tools or maps. You always know which direction is north, the approximate time of day, and your rough position relative to any coastline you've visited before. You **cannot become lost at sea** so long as you can **see the stars and have a map or chart in your inventory**. Without either, you still have advantage on Survival checks to navigate.
- You can **predict sea weather and tidal conditions** for the next 48 hours by reading the water and sky. You know when a storm is coming, when the tide will turn, and when conditions are favorable for travel.
- Once per long rest, you can issue a **rallying cry** to up to 6 allies. For the next 10 minutes, each ally gains **advantage on saving throws against being frightened** and gains **temporary hit points equal to your Charisma modifier + your proficiency bonus**. This represents the bond forged between crew members — the knowledge that you face the storm together.
- When you are **on or near water** (aboard a ship, on a beach, by a river, in rain), you have **advantage on initiative rolls** and your movement speed increases by **10 feet**. The sea gives you energy.

*Reasoning: The Sailor's L11 is "captain." Star navigation is the skill payoff. The rallying cry is the Sailor's version of the Soldier's Rally — but the Soldier's is about military discipline, while the Sailor's is about crew bonds and facing storms together. The water proximity bonus is a terrain-conditional power boost that rewards the Sailor's home turf.*

### L17 — Master of the Deep
**Expertise Die scales to d6** on Perception and Athletics.

- You have **resistance to cold damage** and can **breathe underwater** for up to **30 minutes** per long rest (this is not magic — it's an almost supernatural lung capacity and cold tolerance developed over a lifetime at sea).
- Once per long rest, you can **read the deep currents** — spending 10 minutes in meditation near a body of water, you learn what lies beneath and around it within a **1-mile radius**: submerged structures, large creatures, underwater passages, shipwrecks, and hidden coves. The water tells its secrets to those who know how to listen.
- **Storm's Chosen:** When you are outdoors during a storm or heavy weather, you gain the following: **advantage on Strength and Dexterity saving throws**, your weapon attacks deal an additional **1d6 thunder damage**, and you are immune to the prone condition. The storm empowers you. Once per long rest, while the storm is active, you can invoke a surge of power as a bonus action — for the next **1 minute**, you additionally have **advantage on all attack rolls** and enemies within 5 feet of you have disadvantage on attacks against you. The storm recognizes its own.

*Reasoning: The Sailor's capstone is "the sea chose you." Underwater breathing and cold resistance are the survivability payoffs. Reading the deep currents is the maritime equivalent of the Outlander's land communion — underwater recon. Storm's Chosen is the dramatic capstone — the Sailor who fights hardest when the storm is worst. It's terrain-conditional (only during storms) so it's powerful when it triggers but not always-on. This pairs beautifully with the weather system — the party's Sailor is the one who says "No, DON'T take shelter. This is where I'm strongest."*

## Far Traveler

**Identity:** Someone from a distant land — not just well-traveled, but fundamentally an outsider everywhere they go. The Far Traveler carries the wisdom of many cultures, the ability to adapt to unfamiliar situations, and an outsider's perspective that sees things locals miss.

**Signature Skills:** Insight, Perception

**How it diverges from Outlander:** Outlander knows one wilderness deeply. Far Traveler knows many *cultures* broadly. Outlander's edge is in nature. Far Traveler's edge is in adaptation — they're never truly out of their depth because they've been out of their depth before and learned to read the situation.

### L1 — All Eyes on You (existing)
Your accent, mannerisms, figures of speech, and perhaps even your appearance all mark you as foreign. Curious glances are directed your way wherever you go, which can be a nuisance, but you also gain the hospitality of those who are intrigued by far-off lands. You can parley this attention into access to people and places you might not otherwise have, as the curiosity of the locals works in your favor.

### L5 — Worldly Wisdom
Your travels have taught you to adapt to any culture or environment.
- You can **learn the basics of a new language** (enough to hold simple conversations, ask directions, read signs) after **3 days of immersion** in a community that speaks it. Full fluency requires 30 days.
- After spending **1 hour observing a new culture or community**, you understand their **customs, taboos, social hierarchy, and greeting rituals** well enough to avoid giving offense. You have **advantage on Charisma checks** to make a positive first impression with members of a culture you've studied this way.
- You have **advantage on Wisdom saving throws against being frightened or disoriented** by unfamiliar or alien environments (the Underdark, the Feywild, a foreign plane). You've seen enough strangeness that little truly shocks you anymore.

*Reasoning: The Far Traveler's L5 is "I've been somewhere like this before." Fast language acquisition rewards the player who talks to everyone. Cultural adaptation is a social scouting tool that prevents faux pas. The Wisdom save advantage against alien environments is the Far Traveler's signature composure — even the Feywild doesn't rattle them.*

### L11 — Stranger in Every Land
**Expertise Die (d4)** on Insight and Perception checks.

- You can **spot what's out of place** — when you enter any new location (a room, a building, a settlement), you automatically notice one thing that is **unusual, hidden, or doesn't belong**, if such a thing exists. The DM tells you something is off; you may need to investigate further to learn what. *(AI DM integration note: the system must proactively notify Far Traveler L11+ characters of anything out of place when entering new locations, and suggest investigation when appropriate.)*
- Once per long rest, you can draw on the **wisdom of a distant culture** to solve a problem. Describe the situation to the DM — the DM provides a piece of practical wisdom, a technique, or a custom from one of the many cultures you've visited that is directly applicable. This might be a specific poison antidote known only in a southern desert, a negotiation technique from a far eastern court, or a construction method from mountain-dwelling monks. This grants **advantage on one check** related to applying that knowledge.
- Your **outsider's perspective** makes you resistant to groupthink. You have **advantage on Insight checks** to detect when someone is lying to or manipulating a group (not just you individually — you can spot a demagogue working a crowd or a courtier playing a room).

*Reasoning: The Far Traveler's L11 is "the outsider who sees everything." The auto-notice ability is powerful but narrowly scoped — you notice ONE thing, and only if something is actually off. The distant culture wisdom is a flexible "I know something about this" ability that rewards creative problem solving. The groupthink resistance is the outsider's edge — you're not part of the social fabric, so you see the threads being pulled.*

### L17 — Citizen of the World
**Expertise Die scales to d6** on Insight and Perception.

- **Accelerated fluency:** Full fluency in a new language now requires only **15 days of immersion** (reduced from 30). You can also communicate basic concepts with any intelligent creature, even if you share no language — through gesture, drawing, and an intuitive understanding of universal needs.
- You are **immune to the charmed condition** from non-divine sources. You have encountered too many forms of manipulation across too many cultures to be swayed by mortal enchantment. Divine charm effects still function but you have advantage on the save.
- Once per long rest, you can **call upon a contact from your travels** in any settlement, regardless of whether you've visited it before. A former fellow traveler, a merchant you met on the road, or a scholar you corresponded with — someone who owes you a kindness lives here. The DM determines who they are and what they can offer, but they are **friendly to you** and willing to provide information, shelter, or a favor.
- Your **outsider's perspective** has become prophetic. Once per long rest, you can warn the party of danger — you have **seen this pattern before**. Grant the party **advantage on initiative** for the next encounter, and no member of the party can be **surprised**.

*Reasoning: The Far Traveler's capstone is "you belong everywhere and are beholden to no one." Universal language is the social payoff — at L17, language barriers simply don't exist for you. Charm immunity reflects a mind that has been shaped by so many cultures that no single one can dominate it. The universal contact network is the Far Traveler's infrastructure — Criminal has the underworld, Noble has nobility, Far Traveler has... everyone, everywhere, because they've been everywhere. The danger foresight is the capstone combat utility — the Far Traveler has seen enough ambushes across enough lands to know when one is coming.*

## Haunted One

**Identity:** Someone who has encountered true horror — not adventure-story danger, but something that fundamentally changed how they perceive reality. The Haunted One carries darkness with them. They understand evil, fear, and the supernatural in a way that makes others uncomfortable — but that understanding is also their greatest weapon.

**Signature Skills:** Investigation, Intimidation

**How it diverges from other Themes:** No other Theme deals with *trauma as a source of power*. The Haunted One is the only Theme where the progression is explicitly about turning suffering into strength. The Criminal operates in moral gray areas; the Haunted One has stared into genuine darkness and came back different.

### L1 — Heart of Darkness (existing)
Those who look into your eyes can see that you have faced unimaginable horror, and that you are no stranger to darkness. Though they might fear you, commoners will extend you every courtesy and do their utmost to help you. Unless you have shown yourself to be a danger to them, they will even take up arms to fight alongside you, should you find yourself facing an enemy alone.

### L5 — Mark of Dread
Your encounter with darkness left a permanent mark — one that is both a burden and a weapon.
- You have **advantage on saving throws against being frightened**. You've already faced the worst thing you can imagine; mortal fear struggles to reach you.
- Once per short rest, you can **channel the dread you carry** — as an action, choose one creature within 30 feet that can see you. It must make a **Wisdom saving throw (DC = 8 + proficiency + your choice of Charisma or Wisdom modifier)** or be **frightened of you** for 1 minute. The creature can repeat the save at the end of each of its turns. This is not magic — it's the weight of what you've seen, visible in your eyes.
- You can **sense the presence of undead, fiends, and aberrations** within **60 feet** of you. You don't know their exact location or type, only that something unnatural is nearby. This sense is always active and requires no action.

*Reasoning: The Haunted One's L5 is "the darkness gave me something back." Fear resistance is the survivor's payoff. The dread channel is the Haunted One's signature — they don't intimidate through size or rank, they intimidate by showing you a glimpse of what they've seen. The supernatural sense is a narrow but always-useful radar that makes the Haunted One the party's early warning system for the things that go bump in the night.*

### L11 — Inured to Horror
**Expertise Die (d4)** on Investigation and Intimidation checks.

- You are **immune to being frightened**. Full stop. Nothing mortal or magical can frighten you anymore.
- When you encounter a **curse, haunting, or supernatural phenomenon**, you can spend 10 minutes investigating it and learn **one** of the following (your choice): its **nature** (what kind of entity or magic is responsible), its **anchor** (what sustains it — an object, a corpse, a location, a pact), or one **weakness or method of ending it**. No check required — you recognize the patterns. You can only investigate a given phenomenon once per long rest; further insight requires returning with new information.
- Once per long rest, you can share your **dark knowledge** with an ally. Spend 1 minute telling them what you know about the horror you're facing. Roll a **d10** — on a **1**, the knowledge overwhelms the ally and they instead have **disadvantage on saving throws** against that creature type for the duration (the darkness you carry is contagious). On a **2-10**, the ally gains **advantage on saving throws** against the effects of that specific creature type (undead, fiend, aberration, or the specific curse/haunting) for 1 hour. You also have advantage on attack rolls against that creature type for the same duration regardless of the d10 result.

*Reasoning: The Haunted One's L11 is "I understand the darkness." Full fear immunity is the upgrade from advantage — earned, not given. The curse/haunting analysis is the Haunted One's equivalent of the Sage's Analytical Eye — but where the Sage studies through knowledge, the Haunted One recognizes patterns from direct experience. Learning only one piece per investigation makes the player choose what they need most. Sharing dark knowledge has a 10% backfire chance — the darkness the Haunted One carries isn't always safe to share, and that risk creates a meaningful decision about whether to brief the party or keep it to yourself.*

### L17 — Gaze into the Abyss
**Expertise Die scales to d6** on Investigation and Intimidation.

- You have **resistance to necrotic and psychic damage**. The darkness has tried to consume you and failed; it settles for a grudging stalemate.
- Once per long rest, you can **confront a supernatural entity directly** — you lock eyes with an undead, fiend, or aberration within 30 feet and impose a **battle of wills**. The creature makes a **Wisdom saving throw (DC = 8 + proficiency + Charisma or Wisdom modifier)**. On a failure, choose one:
  - **Banish:** The creature is banished to its plane of origin for 1 minute (as Banishment, but not a spell — it cannot be counterspelled). If the creature is native to this plane, it is instead incapacitated for 1 minute.
  - **Bind:** The creature is paralyzed for 1 minute (save at end of each turn to break free). While paralyzed, it cannot use legendary actions or lair actions.
  - **Break:** The creature's psychic defenses shatter. For the next minute, it is vulnerable to all damage it takes and has disadvantage on all saving throws.
- **Aura of Unease:** Undead, fiends, and aberrations within **30 feet** of you have **disadvantage on attack rolls** against you. They sense what you are — someone who has seen what they are and did not break.

*Reasoning: The Haunted One's capstone is "the abyss looked back, and flinched." Necrotic/psychic resistance is the survivability payoff against the exact damage types these enemies deal. The confrontation is the Haunted One's "Divine Intercessor" equivalent — a once-per-day ability that can completely change an encounter against the right enemy type. Three options give flexibility. The aura of unease is the passive capstone — undead and fiends instinctively know this person is dangerous to them. It's narrowly typed (only works against those creature types) but devastating in context.*

## Guild Artisan

**Identity:** A craftsperson trained within a guild — not just someone who makes things, but someone embedded in a network of trade, reputation, and professional standards. The Guild Artisan understands materials, markets, and the politics of commerce. Their power comes from what they can build, who they know, and the respect their craft commands.

**Signature Skills:** Persuasion, Insight

**How it diverges from Clan Crafter:** Guild Artisan is about *the guild* — commerce, trade networks, professional reputation, urban craft economy. Clan Crafter is about *the craft itself* — ancestral techniques, material mastery, the spiritual relationship between maker and material. Guild Artisan's edge is social and commercial. Clan Crafter's edge is technical and material.

### L1 — Guild Membership (existing)
As an established member of a guild, you can rely on certain benefits that membership provides. Your fellow guild members will provide you with lodging and food if necessary, and pay for your funeral if needed. In some cities and towns, a guildhall offers a central place to meet other members of your profession, which can be a good place to meet potential patrons, allies, or hirelings. Guilds often wield tremendous political power.

### L5 — Artisan's Eye
Your training has given you an intuitive understanding of craftsmanship, quality, and value.
- You can **appraise any object** accurately by handling it for 1 minute — you know its approximate market value, its quality (common, fine, superior, masterwork), whether it's been repaired or modified, and whether it's a forgery or counterfeit. No check required for non-magical items; **DC 15 Intelligence check** for magic items (you can tell it's magical and estimate its rarity, but not identify the specific enchantment).
- You can **identify the maker's origin** of any crafted item — the region, the tradition, and (if the maker was renowned) the specific artisan. This is passive knowledge, not magic.
- When purchasing goods, you have **advantage on Persuasion checks** to negotiate prices, and you cannot be overcharged by more than 10% (you know what things are worth).

*Reasoning: The Guild Artisan's L5 is "I know what things are worth." Appraisal is universally useful — the party looks to the Guild Artisan before buying, selling, or looting. Maker's origin is a nice investigative tool (who made this poison? where was this sword forged?). Price negotiation is the commercial edge — the Guild Artisan saves the party gold every time they shop.*

### L11 — Master Craftsman
**Expertise Die (d4)** on Persuasion and Insight checks.

- When you craft an item using tools you're proficient with, your **crafting time is halved** and you can attempt to create items **one quality tier higher** than the materials would normally allow (a DC 15 check with your artisan's tools; failure produces standard quality, not a wasted attempt).
- Once per long rest, you can perform **field repairs** on a damaged weapon, piece of armor, or piece of equipment. The repair takes 10 minutes and restores the item to full functionality until the next long rest, at which point a proper repair is needed. This works on magical items (you're repairing the physical vessel, not the enchantment).
- Your **guild connections** span multiple cities. In any settlement with a trade guild, you can secure: access to a **workshop and tools** for up to 3 days, introductions to **local merchants and politicians** (advantage on first Charisma check), and **wholesale pricing** on raw materials (25% discount).

*Reasoning: The Guild Artisan's L11 is "master of the trade." Halved crafting time and quality tier upgrades make them the best crafter in the party — this ties directly into the existing crafting system. Field repairs are the practical utility — the Guild Artisan keeps the party's gear functional in the field. Guild connections mirror the Criminal's Underworld Network and the Noble's hospitality — each Theme builds their own infrastructure through a different social lens.*

### L17 — Guildmaster's Legacy
**Expertise Die scales to d6** on Persuasion and Insight.

- You can craft **masterwork items** reliably — when you attempt masterwork quality, you succeed on a roll of **10 or higher** (normally much harder). Your masterwork items are recognized across the realm as works of a true master, and carry your personal maker's mark.
- Once per long rest, you can create a **temporary enchantment** on a nonmagical item you've crafted or repaired. Choose one: **+1 to attack and damage rolls** (weapon), **+1 to AC** (armor), or a **minor utility effect** appropriate to the item (a lantern that doesn't need fuel, boots that leave no tracks, a rope that ties itself). The enchantment lasts **24 hours**. This is not magic — it's craftsmanship so perfect it *approaches* the magical.
- Your reputation as a master artisan grants you **political influence** in any settlement with a trade guild. Guild leaders treat you as an equal, and you can request a **guild vote** on matters affecting the settlement — tariffs, trade agreements, sanctions against a merchant, or guild resources committed to a cause. The outcome depends on your argument (Persuasion check), but you always get the vote.

*Reasoning: The Guild Artisan's capstone is "your craft has transcended the ordinary." Reliable masterwork is the crafter's dream — at L17, your hands produce excellence consistently. The temporary enchantment is the headline — a nonmagical +1 that lasts a day. It's weaker than a permanent magic item but it's free, daily, and flexible. The guild political influence is the social capstone — at this level, you're not just a craftsperson, you're a power broker in the trade world.*

## Clan Crafter

**Identity:** An artisan trained not by a guild but by a *clan* — a family or cultural tradition that treats crafting as heritage, not commerce. The Clan Crafter's relationship with materials is almost spiritual. They know the old techniques, the ancestral secrets, and the way a material wants to be shaped. Their craft is identity, not profession.

**Signature Skills:** History, Investigation

**How it diverges from Guild Artisan:** Guild Artisan's power is social and commercial — guild networks, appraisal, trade politics. Clan Crafter's power is technical and material — they understand *substances* at a deeper level, can work with materials others can't, and their craft carries the weight of ancestral tradition. A Guild Artisan makes a fine sword; a Clan Crafter makes a sword that *means* something.

### L1 — Respect of the Stout Folk (existing)
As well respected as clan crafters are among outsiders, no one esteems them quite so highly as dwarves do. You always have free room and board in any place where shield dwarves or gold dwarves dwell, and the individuals in such a settlement might vie among themselves to determine who can offer you (and possibly your companions) the finest accommodations and assistance.

### L5 — Material Sense
Your clan's traditions have given you an intimate understanding of physical materials.
- You can **identify any material** by touch — metal, wood, stone, bone, hide, fiber, or composite. You know its properties, its purity, its age, and whether it's been alchemically or magically treated. No check required.
- You can **detect structural weaknesses** in objects and structures by examining them for 1 minute. You learn the weakest point (where to strike to break it), its approximate hit points, and its damage threshold if it has one. For structures (walls, doors, bridges), you also learn whether it's safe to traverse.
- You have **advantage on checks** made with any artisan's tools you're proficient with, and you can use **improvised tools** without disadvantage (a rock becomes a hammer, a sharp stone becomes a chisel).

*Reasoning: The Clan Crafter's L5 is "I understand what things are made of." Material identification is the technical counterpart to the Guild Artisan's appraisal — they know the value, you know the substance. Structural weakness detection is unique and useful (where do I hit this door? is this bridge safe?). Improvised tools mean the Clan Crafter is never without their craft.*

### L11 — Ancestral Techniques
**Expertise Die (d4)** on History and Investigation checks.

- You know **3 ancestral crafting techniques** passed down through your clan. Each technique allows you to work with a material category that others cannot:
  - **Stoneshaping:** You can work raw stone as if it were soft clay during crafting, producing joints and shapes that would normally require magical assistance.
  - **Deep Forging:** You can work adamantine and mithral without a magical forge. Items you craft from these materials retain their special properties.
  - **Living Craft:** You can shape wood, bone, and hide without killing or cutting the source material — bending living wood, molding shed antler, weaving naturally fallen fibers. Items created this way are resistant to decay and have advantage on saves against being destroyed.
  *(The DM may allow additional ancestral techniques appropriate to the character's clan tradition.)*
- When you **repair or modify a magical item**, you do not risk damaging its enchantment. You can resize armor, refit a weapon's grip, or mend a cracked staff without disrupting the magic. This requires a successful **DC 15 check** with appropriate tools.
- You can **read the history of a crafted object** by studying it for 10 minutes — you learn who made it (their tradition, skill level, and emotional state during crafting), approximately when it was made, and what it was originally intended for. This is not magic — it's reading the tool marks, the material choices, and the craft tradition.

*Reasoning: The Clan Crafter's L11 is "keeper of the old ways." The three ancestral techniques give them access to crafting methods that are otherwise impossible without magic — this is the Clan Crafter's equivalent of the Criminal's favor network, but expressed through craft rather than social connections. Magical item repair without risk is a huge practical utility. Reading an object's history is the investigative tool — the Clan Crafter picks up the mysterious dagger and says "this was forged in grief, by a dwarf of the Ironpeak tradition, two centuries ago, for a left-handed wielder."*

### L17 — Magnum Opus
**Expertise Die scales to d6** on History and Investigation.

- Once per campaign arc, you can undertake a **Magnum Opus** — a crafting project of legendary scope. Working for at least **7 days** with appropriate materials, you create an item that is **permanently magical**, bearing one enchantment of your choice (equivalent to an uncommon or rare magic item, subject to DM approval). This is not spellcasting — the enchantment arises from the perfection of craft itself. The item bears your clan's mark and becomes part of your legacy.
- **Material Mastery:** Your understanding of materials has become near-supernatural. When you handle any material or object for 1 minute, you learn its **essential nature** — its hidden potential, its resistances and vulnerabilities, and what it *wants* to become in the hands of a master. You can then do one of the following once per long rest:
  - **Temper:** For 24 hours, you grant a material resistance to one of its natural weaknesses (wood resists fire, ice resists heat, steel resists rust, leather resists rot). Useful for making gear survive hostile environments.
  - **Weaken:** When examining an object or structure, you identify the **one perfect strike** to shatter it. The next attack made against that object or structure by you or an ally within 1 minute is treated as a **critical hit with maximum damage** against the object (does not affect creatures).
  - **Extract:** You distill the essence of a material into a usable form — a shard of adamantine's resilience, a drop of dragonscale's fire-resistance, a strand of phoenix-down's warmth. You gain one **crafting reagent** of legendary quality, usable in a future crafting project for significant bonuses (DM determines specific effect).
- **Indestructible craft:** Items you create of **masterwork quality** have resistance to all damage and advantage on all saving throws. They don't rust, rot, or degrade over time. Your work endures.

*Reasoning: The Clan Crafter's capstone is "your hands create things that last forever." The Magnum Opus is the headline — a permanent magic item created through craft, not spell. Once per campaign arc keeps it rare and significant. Material Mastery evolves the L5 Material Sense into a L17 power — the Clan Crafter doesn't just read materials, they *commune* with them, coaxing them to transcend their normal limits. Three choices (Temper/Weaken/Extract) give flexibility for what the moment needs. Indestructible masterwork is the legacy ability — a sword made by a L17 Clan Crafter will outlive kingdoms.*

## Hermit

**Identity:** Someone who withdrew from society to seek truth in solitude. The Hermit isn't just a loner — a Criminal or Outlander might be solitary too. The Hermit deliberately chose isolation as a path to understanding. They've spent years in meditation, contemplation, or private research, and they returned with knowledge that can't be found in any library.

**Signature Skills:** Medicine, Religion

**How it diverges from Sage:** Sage is about *institutional knowledge* — libraries, universities, formal study, accumulated scholarship. Hermit is about *personal revelation* — solitude, meditation, inner truth, discoveries made alone. The Sage consults books; the Hermit consults themselves. The Sage's power comes from breadth of knowledge; the Hermit's power comes from depth of insight.

**How it diverges from Acolyte:** Acolyte is about *communal faith* — temples, congregations, shared ritual, service to others. Hermit is about *solitary truth* — withdrawn from community, possibly from organized religion entirely. The Acolyte draws strength from their flock; the Hermit draws strength from silence.

### L1 — Discovery (existing)
The quiet seclusion of your extended hermitage gave you access to a unique and powerful discovery. The exact nature of this revelation depends on the nature of your seclusion. It might be a great truth about the cosmos, the deities, the conditions of the world, or the forces of nature. It could also be a site that no one else has ever seen, or a fact long thought forgotten. Work with your DM to determine the details of your discovery and its impact on the campaign.

### L5 — Inner Stillness
Years of solitude have given you mastery over your own mind and body.
- You can **meditate** for 10 minutes to gain the benefits of a **short rest** (you don't actually rest — you center yourself). This can only be used **once per long rest** and does not replace the need for actual short rests for hit die recovery; it restores only features that recharge on a short rest.
- You have **advantage on Wisdom saving throws** against being charmed, compelled, or having your thoughts read. Your mind is a fortress of stillness — intrusion is difficult.
- You can **detect the presence of magic** within 30 feet as an action (as the Detect Magic spell, but it is not a spell — it's a meditative attunement to the weave). You sense the presence and school of magic, but must investigate further to identify specific effects.

*Reasoning: The Hermit's L5 is "mastery of the self." The meditation-as-short-rest is a unique resource management tool — once per day, the Hermit resets their short rest features without actually stopping. The Wisdom save advantage is the mental fortress built by years of solitude. Detecting magic through meditation rather than spell slots is the Hermit's version of the Haunted One's supernatural sense — narrower than Detect Magic the spell (no duration, requires an action each time) but free and unlimited.*

### L11 — Revelation
**Expertise Die (d4)** on Medicine and Religion checks.

- Once per long rest, you can enter a **meditative trance** for 10 minutes and receive a **revelation** about a problem, situation, or question facing you. The DM provides one piece of insight that you could not have obtained through normal investigation — a hidden connection, a truth about someone's nature, a warning about a future event, or clarification about a past mystery. This is not divine communication — it's your subconscious mind, trained by years of contemplation, surfacing patterns you couldn't see consciously.
- You can **diagnose and treat ailments of the mind and spirit** that medicine alone cannot reach. Once per long rest, you can spend 10 minutes with a willing creature and cure one of: a **madness effect**, a **psychic curse**, **indefinite madness**, or the **charmed/frightened condition** if it has persisted for more than 24 hours (representing deep-seated magical fear or enchantment, not combat conditions). This requires no check — your presence and guidance are the cure.
- Your **self-sufficiency** is complete. You require only **half the normal food and water** per day, and you can go **twice as long** without sleep before suffering exhaustion. Years of ascetic living have conditioned your body.

*Reasoning: The Hermit's L11 is "seer." The revelation is the Hermit's signature — the AI DM delivers a genuine insight that advances the story, framed as inner vision rather than divine message. Mental/spiritual healing fills a niche no other Theme touches — the party's Hermit is the one who fixes madness and deep curses. Self-sufficiency is the practical payoff of the ascetic life and pairs with the survival system.*

### L17 — Transcendence
**Expertise Die scales to d6** on Medicine and Religion.

- You have **resistance to psychic damage** and are **immune to being charmed and to madness effects**. Your mind has been tempered beyond what mortal influence can reach.
- Once per long rest, you can enter a **state of transcendence** for up to **10 minutes**. When you begin this state, choose one of the following modes (you cannot switch mid-trance):
  - **Clarity of Sight:** You have **truesight out to 60 feet** — you see invisible creatures, see through illusions, detect shapechangers, and perceive the true form of everything around you. This is not magic; it's perception stripped of all assumption.
  - **Projected Awareness:** You project your **consciousness** up to 1 mile from your body, perceiving through an invisible, intangible point. Your body remains in a meditative state and is defenseless. You can see, hear, and understand anything at the projection point, but cannot interact physically.
  - **Unified Mind:** You can communicate **telepathically** with any creature you can see, regardless of language, and you have advantage on all Wisdom and Intelligence checks for the duration.
- Once per campaign arc, your **discovery** (L1 feature) deepens. The DM reveals a new facet of the truth you uncovered during your hermitage — an expansion, a consequence, or a connection to current events that reframes your understanding. This is the Hermit's ultimate payoff: the truth they found in solitude continues to unfold.

*Reasoning: The Hermit's capstone is "you see the world as it truly is." Psychic resistance and charm immunity are the mental fortress completed. Transcendence is the Hermit's equivalent of the Sage's Living Library or the Acolyte's Divine Intercessor — a once-daily mode shift that grants extraordinary perception. Truesight without magic is unique. The discovery deepening ties the entire Theme back to its L1 feature — the thing you found in solitude is still revealing itself at L17, and the DM gets a narrative hook to deliver a campaign-shaping revelation.*

## Investigator

**Identity:** A professional solver of mysteries — not a scholar who studies, but someone who *follows the thread*. The Investigator reads crime scenes, interviews witnesses, connects disparate facts, and thinks like the people they're pursuing. Their power is in observation, deduction, and an almost obsessive attention to detail.

**Signature Skills:** Investigation, Perception

**How it diverges from Sage:** Sage accumulates knowledge broadly. Investigator applies knowledge narrowly to solve specific problems. The Sage reads a book about poisons; the Investigator examines the victim's lips and knows which poison was used. Sage's power is recall. Investigator's power is deduction.

**How it diverges from Criminal:** Criminal operates *within* the underworld. Investigator operates *against* it (or at least observes it from the outside). Criminal knows how to pick a lock; Investigator knows how to tell someone else picked it. They are natural foils.

### L1 — Official Inquiry (existing)
You carry the authority of your position, and have a knack for getting information. You can often get access to places and people others might not be able to, either through your official capacity or by calling in favors. Local authorities and institutions are usually cooperative with your inquiries, unless you've given them reason not to be.

### L5 — Scene Reader
Your trained eye turns any location into a story.
- When you **examine a location** where an event occurred (a crime scene, a battlefield, a campsite, a room where a conversation happened), you can spend **10 minutes** and automatically learn: **how many creatures were present**, **approximately when the event occurred** (within hours for recent, within days for older), and **one specific detail** the DM chooses — a clue that others would miss (a scuff mark, a dropped button, an unusual scent, a pattern in the destruction).
- You have **advantage on Investigation checks** to find hidden objects, secret doors, traps, and concealed compartments. You don't just look — you *search*.
- When you **interview a witness or suspect**, you can ask one **pointed question** per conversation that the DM answers honestly about the person's truthfulness — not whether the statement is true, but whether the person *believes* it to be true and whether they're deliberately withholding information.

*Reasoning: The Investigator's L5 is "I read the room — literally." Scene reading is the signature — it turns the DM's description into mechanical truth. The one DM-chosen detail is a nice hook for the AI DM to plant clues. Investigation advantage is broad but fitting — the Investigator is the best searcher in the party. The interview technique is the social tool — not Insight (reading emotion) but a specific "are you lying to me right now" question that gives a definitive answer once per conversation.*

### L11 — Deductive Mind
**Expertise Die (d4)** on Investigation and Perception checks.

- You can **connect the dots** — once per long rest, you can present the DM with **two or more clues, facts, or observations** you've gathered, and ask how they're connected. The DM reveals one **genuine connection** between them if one exists, or confirms there is no connection. This is deduction, not divination — you're reasoning from evidence.
- When you **observe a creature** for at least 1 minute (or succeed on a **DC 13 Wisdom (Insight) check** in conversation), you can develop a **profile**: you learn their **general alignment** (good/neutral/evil axis), their **primary motivation** in the current situation, and whether they are **under duress or acting freely**.
- You can **reconstruct events** from physical evidence. When you examine a body, a destroyed object, or a damaged area, you learn the **cause of death/destruction**, the **weapon or force used**, the **direction of attack**, and approximately **how skilled the perpetrator was** (amateur, trained, expert, supernatural).

*Reasoning: The Investigator's L11 is "consulting detective." The connect-the-dots ability is the Investigator's signature — it's the "red string board" moment where they lay out the evidence and see the pattern. The DM provides a real connection, not a hint. Profiling gives them a social read that's different from the Acolyte's pastoral counsel or the Far Traveler's outsider perspective — the Investigator reads people as subjects of investigation. Event reconstruction from physical evidence is the CSI ability — dead body, destroyed room, shattered artifact, the Investigator tells you what happened.*

### L17 — Mastermind
**Expertise Die scales to d6** on Investigation and Perception.

- You have **advantage on all Intelligence and Wisdom checks** related to solving a mystery, tracking a suspect, or analyzing evidence. When a problem has a solution, you are the most likely person in the world to find it.
- Once per long rest, you can declare a **theory** about an ongoing mystery, conspiracy, or situation. You present your theory to the DM, who tells you: **how many of your key assertions are correct** (not which ones — just the count). This lets you refine your theory through successive sessions, narrowing in on the truth through deduction.
- **Predictive instinct:** Once per long rest, you can **predict an enemy's next move**. After observing a creature in combat for at least 1 round, you can declare: "They'll do X next." If you're right (the creature intended that action), the party gains **advantage on all saving throws and AC against that action**. If you're wrong, nothing happens. The DM adjudicates honestly based on the creature's AI/personality.
- You are **immune to being deceived by illusions** of 5th level or lower. Your attention to detail is so refined that you automatically perceive inconsistencies in fabricated reality. Illusions of 6th level or higher still affect you, but you have advantage on the save.

*Reasoning: The Investigator's capstone is "nothing escapes you." The broad advantage on mystery-related checks is the passive payoff. The theory-testing mechanic is unique — it gamifies the investigative process across multiple sessions, where the player gets closer to truth each time they test a theory. Predictive instinct is the combat application of deduction — reading an enemy's pattern and calling their move. Illusion immunity is the perception capstone — the Investigator's eye for detail means fake realities can't fool them.*

## City Watch

**Identity:** A civilian peacekeeper of a specific settlement. The City Watch isn't military — a Soldier handles that. The City Watch is someone who walked the same streets for years, who knows every face, every shortcut, every petty scam, and every unspoken rule of their city. Their power is rooted in *place* and *community*.

**Signature Skills:** Athletics, Insight

**How it diverges from Soldier:** Soldier is military — trained for war, serves a nation, fights organized enemies. City Watch is civilian — trained for peacekeeping, serves a settlement, manages drunks and thieves. Soldier's loyalty is to the unit. Watch's loyalty is to the community they patrol.

**How it diverges from Urban Bounty Hunter:** Watch is *proactive and communal* — they maintain order before crime happens, and they know everyone. Bounty Hunter is *reactive and targeted* — they pursue specific people for specific contracts, and they know targets.

### L1 — Watcher's Eye (existing)
Your experience in enforcing the law, even in a relatively quiet place, has given you a distinctive ability. You can find a clue at the scene of a crime or an inconsistency in someone's story, and you can tell if a place has dangerous undercurrents of criminal activity or other tensions.

### L5 — Patrol Instinct
Years of walking the beat have given you an intuitive read on cities and the people in them.
- You have **perfect recall** of your **home city's** layout (chosen at character creation) and the layouts of any cities that fly the **same banner** as your home city (same kingdom, same city-state alliance, or same jurisdiction). You know every street, alley, major building, shift change for local guards, and the general rhythm of these settlements. In unfamiliar cities, you do not have this recall — you're a capable but unfamiliar visitor.
- When you observe a **crowd or public space**, you can identify within 1 minute: **who is armed** (even if concealed), **who looks out of place**, and **who is paying attention to you or your party**. You have **advantage on Perception checks** to spot pickpockets, tails, and ambushes in urban environments.
- You can find a **safe house, watch station, or trustworthy contact** in any settlement of town size or larger within **1d4 hours** — a place to regroup, hide a prisoner, or wait out a storm. This is not through underworld connections like the Criminal; it's through the informal network of fellow watchmen, retired guards, and law-abiding citizens who recognize a peer.

*Reasoning: The City Watch's L5 is "I know every city like I know my own." Perfect recall of urban layouts is the scouting payoff. Reading crowds is the defensive tool — the Watch spots danger before it strikes. The safe-house network is the inverse of the Criminal's underworld — the Watch has a network of the law-abiding, not the law-breaking. This creates interesting party dynamics when a City Watch and a Criminal are in the same party — both have urban networks, but they're completely opposed.*

### L11 — Long Arm of the Law
**Expertise Die (d4)** on Athletics and Insight checks.

- Your authority is recognized across jurisdictions. In any settlement with an organized watch or guard, you can **invoke your office** to gain: access to restricted areas, cooperation from local guards (they provide information and limited assistance), the ability to **detain a suspect for up to 24 hours** without needing formal charges, and **free lodging** at guard barracks or watch stations for up to 3 days.
- Once per long rest, you can **summon backup** — in any urban area, within 1d6 minutes, **2d4 local guards or watchmen** arrive to assist you for up to 1 hour. They follow reasonable orders related to law enforcement (arresting suspects, clearing a building, holding a perimeter) but will not fight in obvious offensive action against lawful targets. **Standard guard stats (for DM reference):** AC 16 (chain shirt + shield), HP 11 (2d8+2), Speed 30 ft., STR 13, DEX 12, CON 12, INT 10, WIS 11, CHA 10, Perception +2, spear +3 to hit for 1d6+1 piercing (melee) or 1d8+1 piercing (thrown), longbow +3 to hit for 1d8+1 piercing. Equivalent to the standard "Guard" creature from the Monster Manual.
- **Reputation among peers:** Watchmen in any city have heard of you. You have **advantage on Charisma checks** with law enforcement, **disadvantage on Stealth checks** to avoid being recognized by watchmen or guards in settlements where your reputation has spread.

*Reasoning: The City Watch's L11 is "institutional authority." Cross-jurisdictional office is the Watch's version of the Noble's political access — but earned through service rather than birth. Summoning backup is a powerful situational ability — you can turn a fight into a fight with 2d4 guards on your side, as long as you're in a city and the enemies are legally pursuable. The reputation trade-off mirrors the Entertainer's fame — respected by peers, harder to move unnoticed. It's thematically perfect: the Watch *is* the public face of law.*

### L17 — Keeper of the Peace
**Expertise Die scales to d6** on Athletics and Insight.

- You can **sense threats to public order** within any settlement you're in. Once per long rest, spending 10 minutes on a rooftop, tower, or high vantage point, you perceive the settlement as a whole — you learn the **locations of any active crimes in progress**, any **planned violence or uprising** within the next 24 hours, and any **significant threats (monsters, fugitives, magical disturbances)** in or near the city. This is the culmination of decades of pattern recognition — you feel the city's pulse.
- Your authority has grown beyond a single city. In the region where you've served (DM-determined — typically a kingdom, city-state, or comparable area), you can **issue a writ** once per campaign arc, compelling local authorities to: investigate a specific crime, release a prisoner, deploy guards to a location, or grant you access to restricted archives and prisoners. Your writ carries legal weight and cannot be casually ignored.
- **Commanding Presence (urban only):** When you are in a **settlement** and identify yourself as a watchman while issuing a lawful command ("Halt!" "Drop your weapons!" "Disperse!"), hostile creatures within 30 feet that can hear and understand you must make a **Wisdom save (DC = 8 + proficiency + Charisma modifier)** or become **unable to take hostile actions** against you or visible innocents for 1 round. On a successful save, they are immune to this effect for 24 hours. This ability does not function in wilderness, dungeons, or outside civilization — your authority comes from the city, and outside of it, your badge means nothing.

*Reasoning: The City Watch's capstone is "embodiment of civic authority." City-wide threat sensing is the ultimate scouting ability — you know when something is wrong in the city before anyone reports it. The regional writ is the campaign-level political tool, mirroring the Noble's capstone political favor but scoped to law enforcement rather than nobility. Commanding Presence is the social-combat capstone — a legal command that can stop violence in its tracks, once per combat and not on anyone who's saved against it. It's narrow but devastating in the right context.*

## Knight of the Order

**Identity:** A sworn member of a chivalric, religious, or philosophical order. The Knight isn't just a warrior (classes handle that) and isn't just noble by birth (that's Noble). The Knight's power comes from their **oath** and their **order** — they represent something larger than themselves, and that something provides backing, meaning, and a code that shapes their choices.

**Signature Skills:** Persuasion, History

**Order type (chosen at character creation):** The Knight selects the nature of their order: **Chivalric** (martial honor, protecting the weak), **Religious** (divine service, militant faith), **Scholarly** (guardians of knowledge, philosophical truth), or **Secret** (hidden purpose, shadowy work for a higher cause). The order type shapes the flavor and some specific abilities throughout the progression.

**How it diverges from Noble:** Noble's power is *birthright* — unearned social rank. Knight's power is *oath* — earned through service and binding commitment. A Noble can be deposed; a Knight carries their order's authority regardless of politics.

**How it diverges from Soldier:** Soldier serves a *nation's military*; Knight serves an *order that transcends nations*. The order may span multiple kingdoms, answer to no king, or serve a philosophical/religious purpose beyond state loyalty.

### L1 — Knightly Regard (existing)
You receive shelter and succor from members of your knightly order and those who are sympathetic to its aims. If your order is a religious one, you can gain aid from temples and other religious communities of your deity. Fellow knights will grant you lodging and food, and pay for your funeral if needed. In some cases, your order will pay a ransom to free you from captivity, but only if it can't be avoided.

### L5 — Oath Unbroken
Your commitment to your order manifests as real strength.
- While you remain **true to your order's code** (as defined at character creation in consultation with the DM), you gain the following benefits:
  - **Advantage on saving throws against being charmed, frightened, or magically compelled** to act against your code
  - **Advantage on Persuasion checks** when speaking as a representative of your order or its values
- You can **recognize members of your order** on sight (through signs, mannerisms, or heraldry others wouldn't notice), and they recognize you. Order members who encounter you in the field will provide aid within their means.
- You have access to your order's **repository of knowledge**. Once per long rest, you can consult your memory of your order's teachings to recall a **piece of specialized lore** appropriate to your order type (chivalric histories, religious doctrine, philosophical principles, or secret society knowledge). The DM provides truthful, detailed information relevant to a current situation.

**Code of the Order — the oath is complex:** Your Theme abilities are rooted in your relationship with your order's code, but that relationship is rarely simple. The world tests knights constantly — orders grow corrupt, commanders issue unjust commands, old ideals erode into new hypocrisies. Your Theme supports *all* the paths a knight can walk, with distinct mechanical consequences for each. The AI DM tracks your choices and shifts your abilities accordingly.

**The True Path — Uphold the Code (positive consequences):**
When you uphold your order's code faithfully, your Theme abilities function as written, and you periodically gain **recognition**: small rewards from your order (a gift of gold, a commendation, an introduction to a powerful ally, or a piece of order-specific equipment). The AI DM delivers these as narrative moments roughly once per campaign arc, shaped by your deeds.

**The Reformer's Path — Your Order Has Fallen (positive consequences):**
When your order becomes corrupt or betrays its founding ideals, and you **stand by the original code** even as the institution abandons it, you become a **living vessel** of the true oath. Your abilities transfer their source from the *institution* to the *ideal*:
- Your Oath Unbroken saving throw advantage still applies — because you remain true to what your order *should* be
- Other knights who share your concerns recognize you and may secretly aid you. At L11, your "summon a fellow knight" ability calls **like-minded order members** willing to defy corrupted leadership
- You gain a new ability: **Voice of the True Ideal** — once per long rest, you can invoke the order's original principles to a member of the corrupted order. They must make a Wisdom save (DC = 8 + proficiency + Charisma modifier) or spend 1 round in doubt, unable to take hostile actions as they confront their own complicity. On a save, they are immune for 24 hours but remember the words.

**The Martyr's Path — You Refused an Atrocity (positive consequences):**
When your order commands you to commit something monstrous and you **refuse**, the refusal itself becomes a source of power. You may be exiled, branded, or hunted, but you gain:
- **Righteous Conviction:** Your Oath Unbroken saving throw advantage *strengthens* — you now have **advantage on all saving throws** against being charmed, frightened, or magically compelled when standing by the refusal. Your conviction is magical in its intensity
- **Banner of Conscience:** Your L11 Banner ability still functions, but now it rallies those who recognize your moral courage rather than those loyal to the order. Commoners, refugees, and moral dissenters across the realm may seek you out, offering shelter and information. You may accumulate **informal followers** over time — people who believe in what you refused to do
- **Enemies become allies:** Creatures who were enemies of your now-corrupted order may become potential allies, recognizing that you fought against the same darkness they did. The AI DM should surface these moments

**The Complicit Path — You Obeyed Out of Fear (morally complex consequences):**
When your order commands atrocity and you **comply** — whether from loyalty, fear, or the terror of losing all you hold dear — you keep your abilities. You do not lose them. But they *change*:
- Your **Oath Unbroken** benefits remain, but the saving throw advantage becomes shadowed — you succeed, but something hollow lingers behind your eyes. The AI DM may describe growing paranoia, haunting dreams, or a dissonance that other characters sense
- You gain **Dread Authority:** Your Persuasion checks when invoking your order's name gain a darker edge. You automatically succeed in **intimidation** but automatically *fail* to earn genuine trust from good-aligned NPCs. People obey you because they fear you, not because they respect you
- Your **Banner rally** at L11 shifts: the ability still works, but allies rallied under a stained banner take **1 psychic damage per round** from the spiritual wrongness they sense. They fight harder out of desperation, not faith
- **Weight of Complicity:** Once per session, the DM may present you with a moral test — a chance to atone for what you've done, or compound the darkness. Choosing to atone unlocks the Redemption arc. Choosing to compound it drives you toward **the Fallen Path** (below). This is tragic hero territory — your power is real, but it costs you

**The Fallen Path — You Embraced the Corruption:**
If you repeatedly compound complicity, or openly break your code in service of selfish gain (not under duress), your Theme abilities transform fully:
- You **lose** Oath Unbroken benefits and the ability to rally allies under your banner
- You **gain** access to **Dark Authority** — your Intimidation and Persuasion gain an Expertise Die one tier earlier than normal (d4 at L5 instead of L11), but you have disadvantage on Charisma checks with any creature of good alignment who knows of your deeds
- You may be hunted by your former order, but you may also be recruited by darker institutions that value your ruthlessness

**The Redemption Arc — The Fallen Return:**
A knight who has fallen, complied, or broken can seek redemption. This is a **narrative arc**, not a mechanical grind. It involves: public confession or atonement, sacrifices that cost the character something meaningful, and the slow regathering of broken trust. When the arc completes (DM-determined), your abilities return — but reshaped. You may have **Scars of the Fall**: a permanent reminder of what you did and what you earned back. The AI DM should treat redemption as one of the richest stories this Theme enables.

*Design philosophy: The "Knight of the Order" is fantasy's most morally complex archetype. A good Theme shouldn't punish nuance — it should reward the player for engaging with it. Every path here has both costs and powers, and every path enables a distinct kind of roleplay. The True Knight, the Reformer, the Martyr, the Complicit, the Fallen, and the Redeemed are all valid characters, and the AI DM shifts the mechanical expression of the Theme to match the story being told.*

*Reasoning: The Knight's L5 is "the oath is my strength" — and the strength is conditional. The consequences of betraying the code create real roleplay stakes, but they're framed as **opportunity** rather than **pure punishment**. A fallen knight is a compelling character; the mechanics support that arc. Recognition among order members is the network. The lore recall is the Sage-lite ability, narrowed to the order's specialty.*

### L11 — Banner of the Order
**Expertise Die (d4)** on Persuasion and History checks.

- You can **rally allies under the banner of your order**. Once per long rest, as an action, you raise your order's standard (which may be a literal banner, a holy symbol, a scholarly emblem, or a hidden sign). For 1 minute:
  - **Allies within 60 feet** who can see you gain **advantage on saving throws**, **immunity to being frightened**, and when they reduce an enemy to 0 HP, they may **move up to 10 feet as a free action**.
  - **Enemies of your order** within 60 feet (creatures explicitly opposed to your order's cause — rival orders, heretics, sworn foes of your cause) have **disadvantage on saving throws against being frightened** and take a **-1 penalty to attack rolls** against you and your banner-blessed allies.
  - **Creatures indifferent to your order** (common folk, neutral NPCs, unaligned creatures) are unaffected by the banner either way — the banner's power stems from the weight of belief, and those who don't know or care about your order are untouched.
  The effect ends early if you fall unconscious or if the banner is destroyed.
- You can **summon a fellow knight of your order** once per campaign arc. In any settlement where your order has a presence (common for major orders, rare for secret ones), you can send word that you need aid. Within 1d4 days, a **fellow order member** arrives — a competent NPC of approximately your level, sworn to assist you for up to 7 days before returning to their duties. Their combat stats and abilities are determined by the DM based on your order's nature.
- **Order's Voice:** When acting in your order's name, your words carry the weight of institutional authority. You have **advantage on Charisma checks** to invoke obligations, debts, or historical agreements related to your order. Powerful entities who have dealt with your order before know you speak for it.

*Reasoning: The Knight's L11 is "I am a banner others can rally around." The banner effect is the big party-wide combat buff — mirroring the Soldier's Rally and the Entertainer's Anthem of Valor, but with the distinctive "immunity to fear" tied to the order's spiritual weight. Summoning a fellow knight is the unique network ability — once per arc, an NPC ally joins you temporarily. Order's Voice is the social extension.*

### L17 — Paragon of the Order
**Expertise Die scales to d6** on Persuasion and History.

- You have become a **living exemplar** of your order's ideals. Your mere presence is inspirational: allies within **15 feet** of you have **advantage on saving throws against being charmed, frightened, or compelled**. Additionally, once per long rest, you can **channel the ideal** as a bonus action — for the next **1 minute**, allies within 15 feet additionally gain **+1 to AC and attack rolls** while they can see you. The passive aura is always active; the offensive/defensive boost is a limited resource.
- Once per long rest, you can invoke your order's deepest tradition — a **Sacred Vow** — binding yourself to a specific cause for up to 24 hours. Choose one: **Protect** (grant an ally resistance to all damage for 1 hour), **Judge** (mark a creature as your order's declared enemy — you have advantage on all attack rolls and ability checks against them for 24 hours, and they have disadvantage on saves against your abilities), or **Redeem** (a hostile creature must make a Wisdom save vs your Persuasion DC or regard you and your party as neutral rather than hostile for 1 hour — they will not attack unless attacked, and will listen to reason).
- **Voice of the Order:** Once per campaign arc, your order itself responds to a crisis. You can call upon your order to **commit significant resources** to a cause you champion — a full chapter of knights dispatched to war, a library of scholars unleashed on a research crisis, a religious miracle invoked against a dire threat, or a secret operation launched against a hidden enemy. The scope and success depend on your standing and the situation, but the order *answers when you call*.

*Reasoning: The Knight's capstone is "I am what my order stands for." The aura is the always-on inspiration — allies just feel braver and more capable near you. Sacred Vow's three options mirror the Acolyte's Divine Intercessor but with different thematic flavor: Protect is martial guardianship, Judge is righteous wrath, Redeem is the chivalric "mercy with a blade at the neck." Voice of the Order is the campaign-level payoff — the Knight's most powerful ability isn't personal, it's organizational. When the Knight calls, armies move.*

## Mercenary Veteran

**Identity:** A sellsword who has survived more battles than most. The Mercenary Veteran isn't loyal to any flag, crown, or cause — only to the contract and their own survival. Their power is in ruthless pragmatism, battlefield awareness, and a thousand small tricks learned in countless skirmishes.

**Signature Skills:** Athletics, Persuasion

**How it diverges from Soldier:** Soldier fights for a nation, a unit, a cause. Mercenary Veteran fights for gold. Soldier leads and protects. Mercenary Veteran *survives* and *profits*. Soldier's power comes from discipline and camaraderie; Mercenary Veteran's comes from pragmatism and self-interest.

### L1 — Mercenary Life (existing)
You know the mercenary life as only someone who has lived it can. You are able to identify mercenary companies by their emblems, and you know a little bit about any such company, including the names and reputations of the most notable commanders and leaders, and what sorts of contracts they commonly take. You can find such work for yourself and your companions, often on short notice.

### L5 — Contract Read
You've survived this long because you know how to read a situation for its real stakes.
- Before accepting or engaging in any mission, job, or quest, you can spend 10 minutes assessing the situation and the parties involved. Choose **two** of the following four pieces of information to learn — even a veteran can't read every angle of a deal at once:
  - The **likely hidden dangers** of the mission
  - Whether the **offered reward is fair or lowballed** for the work
  - Whether the **client is being truthful about the mission's scope**
  - Whether there are **rival parties** interested in the same contract
  The DM provides the chosen information based on what a seasoned mercenary would notice. You can only use this ability once per potential contract — you have to pick which questions matter most before committing.
- You have **advantage on Persuasion and Deception checks** when negotiating compensation, contract terms, or payment. People who've tried to stiff mercenaries before learn not to try it with you.
- You can **appraise combat risk** on sight. When encountering a new enemy or group, you can make a **DC 12 Wisdom check** to learn their approximate relative strength (easy/moderate/dangerous/deadly compared to your party), and whether they appear to be professional soldiers, trained warriors, amateurs, or something non-martial.

*Reasoning: The Mercenary Veteran's L5 is "I read the job before I take it." Contract Read is a scouting tool for missions — the party's Mercenary sniffs out the catch before they're committed. Negotiation advantage is the commercial edge. Combat risk appraisal is a battlefield intelligence tool that pairs nicely with the Sage's Analytical Eye (one reads the client, the other reads the creature).*

### L11 — Battlefield Opportunist
**Expertise Die (d4)** on Athletics and Persuasion checks.

- You've survived enough battles to know when one is lost. Once per long rest, when combat goes badly, you can **execute a tactical withdrawal**: you and up to 3 allies within 30 feet can move up to double their speed in one direction without provoking opportunity attacks. Additionally, you have advantage on any Stealth or Survival checks to evade pursuit for the next hour.
- You have an eye for **spoils**. After combat, you automatically identify the most valuable **visible or reasonably concealed** item on the battlefield — things in plain sight, in open pouches, on belts, or in common hiding places (boot tops, sleeve pockets, beneath a cloak). Items hidden in truly clever or magical caches still require a check to find. You can loot a fallen enemy as a bonus action rather than an action.
- **Opportunist's Strike:** Once per short rest, when an enemy within 5 feet of you misses you with a melee attack, you can use your reaction to make one melee weapon attack against them with **advantage**. This represents the mercenary's trained instinct to capitalize on any mistake.

*Reasoning: The Mercenary's L11 is "I know when to run, when to loot, and when to strike." Tactical withdrawal is the survival signature — while the Soldier rallies the party to fight harder, the Mercenary gets them out alive. Spoils identification feeds the commercial instinct (and is genuinely useful in loot-heavy games). Opportunist's Strike is a small combat ability that fits the pragmatic identity — counter-attack on a miss.*

### L17 — Death's Veteran
**Expertise Die scales to d6** on Athletics and Persuasion.

- You have outlived everyone who tried to kill you. You have **resistance to physical damage (bludgeoning, piercing, slashing) from nonmagical attacks**, and when you drop to 0 HP, you can use your reaction to instead drop to **1 HP**. This "nine lives" ability recharges after a long rest.
- You possess **an unmatched network of former comrades**. Once per campaign arc, you can call upon your reputation to assemble a **small mercenary force** — 3d6 competent mercenaries (DM determines statistics, typically equivalent to guards or veterans) who will fight alongside you for one significant engagement, provided you pay them fair market rate (the DM determines cost based on the task). They're not friends — they're professionals who respect you.
- **Dead Eye:** You've killed so many enemies that you instinctively know **exactly how to end a fight**. Once per long rest, you can declare a **killing strike** against an enemy you can see. Your next attack against them is made with **advantage**, and if it hits, it deals **maximum damage** (all variable damage dice treated as their maximum result). **If the attack misses, the ability is expended** — there is no second chance. This is a gamble: the experienced killer betting everything on one perfect strike.

*Reasoning: The Mercenary Veteran's capstone is "I am impossible to kill because I refuse to die." Resistance to nonmagical physical damage and the drop-to-1-HP reaction are the core survivability payoffs — fitting the "survived countless battles" identity. The mercenary force is the unique network ability — not friends, not allies, but paid professionals who respect your reputation. Dead Eye is the combat payoff — a guaranteed hit becomes maximum damage once per day, and if you miss, you don't lose the ability. It's the experienced killer's moment of certainty: "this shot ends it."*

## Urban Bounty Hunter

**Identity:** A hunter of people, not beasts. The Urban Bounty Hunter operates in cities, crowds, and social networks — not wilderness. They find targets through information, patience, and an instinctive read on human behavior. Their power is in *pursuit*: once you become their mark, they don't stop.

**Signature Skills:** Insight, Stealth

**How it diverges from Criminal:** Criminal operates *in* the underworld. Bounty Hunter operates *against* targets within it. Criminal's contacts are fellow criminals. Bounty Hunter's contacts are informants, authorities, and the occasional criminal willing to sell someone out. They share urban skills but with opposite purposes — one hides from the law, the other pursues on its behalf (or for private pay).

**How it diverges from Outlander (who tracks):** Outlander tracks through nature, reading land and beast signs. Bounty Hunter tracks through cities, reading crowds and human behavior. Outlander is a master of one biome; Bounty Hunter is a master of many cities.

### L1 — Ear to the Ground (existing)
You are in frequent contact with people in the segment of society that your chosen quarries move through. These people might be associated with the criminal underworld, the rough-and-tumble folk of the streets, or members of high society. This connection comes in the form of a contact in any city you visit, a person who provides information about people and places of the local area.

### L5 — Mark the Quarry
Once you know you're hunting someone, your focus narrows to a point.
- When you formally "mark" a quarry (declaring your intent to find or capture a specific named target), you gain the following benefits while pursuing them:
  - **Advantage on Investigation checks** to follow their trail, find clues about their whereabouts, or piece together their movements
  - **Advantage on Insight checks** to detect lies or evasions from anyone who knows the target
  - The ability to **sense when you are within 1 mile** of your marked quarry (though not their exact direction)
- You can only have **one active mark at a time**. Marking a new quarry releases the previous one (useful for picking one's battles). Removing a mark voluntarily requires a full day of not pursuing — you must let the target go.

*Reasoning: The Bounty Hunter's L5 is "the hunt is personal." The mark mechanic is the signature — a targeted buff system where the Bounty Hunter gains bonuses specifically against their current quarry. The 1-mile proximity sense is a flavorful "hunter's instinct" that prevents the Bounty Hunter from losing a target completely in a city. The single-mark limitation is the discipline constraint — a Bounty Hunter who chases every bounty catches none.*

### L11 — Relentless Pursuit
**Expertise Die (d4)** on Insight and Stealth checks.

- Against your **marked quarry**, you gain additional benefits:
  - **Advantage on attack rolls** during your first round of combat after locating them
  - Your movement is never impeded by crowds, traffic, or urban obstacles when pursuing them (you slip through with uncanny ease)
  - You can **track them across jurisdictional lines** — your network of contacts and informants extends into any settlement of village size or larger, and you can **pick up a cold trail** up to **30 days old**
- **Informant network:** In any settlement you've visited before (or that has a population of 5,000+), you can acquire information about a specific individual within **1d6 hours**. You learn their general location, their recent activities, who they've been meeting, and whether anyone else is looking for them. The information costs **10 gp × 1d6** in bribes and drinks, with the actual cost determined at the time of purchase.
- You are **difficult to shake**. Creatures attempting to lose you through urban pursuit have **disadvantage on Stealth checks** against your Perception. You have a hunter's patience — you can wait, you can circle back, and you will find them again.

*Reasoning: The Bounty Hunter's L11 is "I don't stop." The enhanced benefits against the marked quarry make the hunt decisive when you catch up. The 30-day cold trail is massive utility — the Bounty Hunter can pick up trails the party thought were lost. Informant network is the commercial infrastructure (costs gold, delivers information). The anti-stealth passive is the hunter's edge — prey can run, but not easily.*

### L17 — No Escape
**Expertise Die scales to d6** on Insight and Stealth.

- Once per long rest, when you mark a quarry, you can designate them as your **prime quarry** for 24 hours. During this time:
  - You **always know the general direction and distance** of your prime quarry, provided they are on the **same continent** as you, or within **500 miles by sea**. Beyond that range, the connection fades until they return within it.
  - Your prime quarry cannot gain the benefits of being **invisible, disguised, or polymorphed** against you; you see through these concealments with your eyes alone
  - You have **advantage on all checks and attack rolls** related to finding, capturing, or confronting them
- **Reputation as the Hunter:** Your name is known among those who make enemies. Creatures who have heard of you (DM determines; at this level, most criminals, nobles, and politicians in civilized lands) must make a **Wisdom save (DC = 8 + proficiency + Charisma or Wisdom modifier)** when they first see you or learn you're hunting them. On a failure, they become **frightened of you** for 1 minute, and have **disadvantage on Deception checks** against you for the next 24 hours. This is not magic — it's genuine reputation.
- **Capture Specialist:** When you attempt to subdue a creature (rather than kill them), you can deal damage that reduces them to 0 HP as **nonlethal**, and you have **advantage on Athletics checks** to grapple, restrain, or bind them. Your grapples and restraints are **DC 15 + your Strength modifier** to escape, reflecting decades of knowing exactly how to hold a prisoner.

*Reasoning: The Bounty Hunter's capstone is "there is nowhere you can hide from me." The prime quarry ability is the headline — once per day, make the hunt inescapable. The DM narrates the target's increasingly desperate attempts to evade you, but they know, and you know, it's only a matter of time. The reputation fear is the passive capstone — everyone in the criminal world knows your name, and that alone shakes them. Capture Specialist is the technical payoff — the Bounty Hunter can take a target alive more reliably than anyone else, which is usually the actual objective of a bounty.*

## Folk Hero

**Identity:** Someone who stood up for the common folk and became a local legend. Not through birth (that's the Noble), not through institutional oath (that's the Knight), not through spectacle (that's the Entertainer) — through a single brave act that caught the imagination of ordinary people. The Folk Hero's power is the **love of the common folk**, and that love is not a ribbon. It's an army that doesn't know it's an army yet.

**Signature Skills:** Animal Handling, Survival

**How it diverges from Knight of the Order:** Knight serves an institution and its code. Folk Hero serves the people directly, and answers to no one but their own conscience. A Knight's banner is their order's standard; a Folk Hero's banner is their own face, recognized and trusted by those who see them as one of their own.

**How it diverges from Entertainer:** Entertainer is famous for their *art*. Folk Hero is famous for their *deeds*. The Entertainer's fame opens doors; the Folk Hero's legend opens *hearts*. Commoners admire the Entertainer from afar; they would *die for* the Folk Hero.

### L1 — Rustic Hospitality (existing)
Since you come from the ranks of the common folk, you fit in among them with ease. You can find a place to hide, rest, or recuperate among other commoners, unless you have shown yourself to be a danger to them. They will shield you from the law or anyone else searching for you, though they will not risk their lives for you.

### L5 — Voice of the People
Your legend has spread through the word of mouth that connects farms, villages, and working districts. In your **home region** (the area where your original heroic deed occurred, roughly a 200-mile radius), you are **already a known figure** — people recognize your face, tell stories about what you did, and point you out to their children. Beyond your home region, your fame is still growing.
- When you enter any settlement **within your home region**, you are automatically recognized by the common folk. They greet you warmly, offer free meals or lodging when they can afford it, and share local news and rumors eagerly.
- When you enter any settlement, you can spend 10 minutes talking with locals and quickly identify **who is struggling, who is in power, and who is exploiting whom**. The common folk tell you things they would never tell an outsider because they know which side you're on.
- You have **advantage on Charisma checks** with commoners, laborers, farmers, artisans, and servants — **provided you have not given them reason to distrust you**. The AI DM tracks your deeds: cruelty to servants, siding with nobility against farmers, ignoring common suffering, or betraying someone's trust can erode this advantage until amends are made. Trust is earned and maintained; it is not a guarantee.
- Once per long rest, you can make an **inspirational appeal** to a commoner or group of commoners. Make a **Charisma (Persuasion) check (DC 12)**. On a success, they agree to take a small personal risk on your behalf — hiding you for the night, delivering a message at risk of discovery, speaking up against local authority, or providing meaningful aid. On a failure, they wish you well but will not endanger themselves. They will never fight in organized combat for you at this tier.

*Reasoning: The Folk Hero's L5 is "the people see you as their own — but you have to earn and keep that status." Automatic recognition in the home region means the Folk Hero *already* feels legendary from L5, rather than waiting for dice rolls to tell them they're famous. The commoner advantage is broad but conditional: a Folk Hero who abuses that trust can lose it, which makes the character accountable to their identity. Unlike the Entertainer's crowd-reading (which is about mood), the Folk Hero's reading is specifically about *power dynamics* — who is oppressed, who is oppressing.*

### L11 — Legend in the Making
**Expertise Die (d4)** on Animal Handling and Survival checks.

- Your deeds are now told around campfires and tavern hearths across the region. Your fame extends by geography:
  - In your **home region** (established at L5): you are a local legend. Free meals, offered lodging, songs sung in your honor, and active aid from commoners in small ways
  - In **adjacent regions** (2-3 regions beyond your home): you are known by reputation and celebrated. Commoners are kindly disposed, share rumors eagerly, and will offer modest help (a meal, a night's lodging, local directions)
  - In **distant lands** (across mountain ranges, seas, or far frontiers): your deeds are known as distant rumors. Common folk are curious and warmly disposed, but haven't heard the full story
  - In **foreign cultures or other planes**: your fame has not traveled here. You are unknown.
  As the campaign progresses and you accumulate new deeds, the AI DM expands your home region outward. Performing new heroic acts in new places plants your legend in those places too.
- Once per long rest, you can **raise a small militia** — in any rural settlement where you are at least *recognized* (d20 roll of 9+), you can rally **3d6 armed commoners** (pitchforks, hunting bows, tool-weapons) to aid you for one significant task lasting up to 12 hours. They are not trained warriors — they're farmers and craftsmen with improvised weapons — but there are many of them and they are **fighting for someone they love**. **Commoner militia stats (for DM reference):** AC 10 (none), HP 4 (1d8), STR 10, DEX 10, CON 10, club or pitchfork +2 to hit for 1d4 bludgeoning/piercing. Equivalent to the standard "Commoner" from the Monster Manual, but armed.
- **Inspirational Presence:** Common folk within 30 feet who can see and hear you have **advantage on saving throws against being frightened**. The Folk Hero's calm gives ordinary people courage they didn't know they had.

*Reasoning: The Folk Hero's L11 is "I am becoming legend." The d20 recognition table is flavorful and gives the AI DM a clear mechanic — the Folk Hero's fame grows organically, sometimes they're known, sometimes not. The militia is the unique institutional-scale ability — where the Knight calls fellow knights and the Noble calls soldiers, the Folk Hero calls *farmers with pitchforks*. They're weak individually but numerous and devoted. Inspirational Presence is the passive support — commoners near the Folk Hero are braver than they have any right to be.*

### L17 — Hero of the Age
**Expertise Die scales to d6** on Animal Handling and Survival.

- Your legend is **known across the realm**. The d20 recognition table at L11 is replaced: in any rural, working-class, or folk settlement, you are automatically recognized (as if rolling a 15+). In urban centers and noble courts, you are **known by reputation** — common folk celebrate you, while those in power view you with a mix of respect, suspicion, and fear.
- Once per campaign arc, you can **incite an uprising** — in a region suffering under tyranny, injustice, or crisis, you can rally the common folk to collective action. This is a narrative event, not a single combat: over days or weeks, workers go on strike, soldiers desert, farmers refuse to pay tribute, and the people become ungovernable. The outcome depends on the DM's determination of local conditions and your cause's justice, but the power you wield is genuine — you cannot easily control what you unleash, but it *will* unleash. (This ability may or may not resolve in your favor. Uprisings are dangerous things, and the Folk Hero who incites one bears responsibility for what follows.)
- **Legend's Shield:** Your name protects you. Any attempt by nobles, authorities, or tyrants to imprison, execute, or quietly disappear you provokes a **regional outcry** — the common folk rise in your defense, and any attempt to punish you publicly has a **75% chance** of causing a rebellion in that settlement. Even corrupt authorities hesitate to move against a Folk Hero. This is not magical protection — it is the weight of popular love, and the fear tyrants feel of it. Private assassination remains possible; public execution is nearly impossible.

*Reasoning: The Folk Hero's capstone is "you are the people's weapon, and they know it." Universal recognition among common folk is the passive social capstone. Inciting an uprising is the campaign-scale power — it mirrors the Knight's "Voice of the Order" and the Noble's "major political favor," but through the lens of organic popular power. The DM warning about uncontrollable consequences is deliberate — the Folk Hero can't just casually start peasant revolts without weighing what comes after. Legend's Shield is the passive capstone protection — you can't be quietly killed by tyrants because the people would notice and respond.*

## Urchin

**Identity:** Someone who survived the streets as a child — not an insider of the underworld (that's the Criminal), not beloved by the common folk (that's the Folk Hero), but someone who lived in the gaps, ignored by society, who learned to be *small, quick, underestimated, and invisible*. The Urchin's power is that no one takes them seriously — and that is the greatest advantage a survivor can have.

**Signature Skills:** Sleight of Hand, Stealth

**How it diverges from Criminal:** Criminal operates *within* a network of criminals — they have a community, contacts, and a profession. Urchin has *none of that*. They survived alone or with other children, outside even the criminal community. The Criminal is a professional; the Urchin is a survivor. The Criminal's contacts are fellow criminals; the Urchin's contacts are *other street children who grew up*.

**How it diverges from Folk Hero:** Folk Hero is *seen* by the common folk — they are a celebrated figure. Urchin is *unseen* by everyone — they are the person who is forgotten, overlooked, not worth noticing. Their invisibility *is* their power. A Folk Hero walks into a tavern and everyone stands. An Urchin walks into the same tavern and no one looks up — and that is exactly what they want.

### L1 — City Secrets (existing)
You know the secret patterns and flow to cities and can find passages through the urban sprawl that others would miss. When you are not in combat, you (and companions you lead) can travel between any two locations in the city twice as fast as your speed would normally allow.

### L5 — Survivor's Instincts
Years of sleeping in alleyways and running from larger predators have given you a feral awareness.
- You can **squeeze through spaces and move at full speed through difficult terrain** made of urban debris (crowds, trash, broken furniture, narrow alleys). Creatures significantly larger than you cannot easily follow you through these spaces.
- Once per short rest, when a creature of a size larger than yours would hit you with an attack, you can use your reaction to **duck or scramble aside**, gaining **+5 AC against that attack**. Large predators have always tried to hit you; you've always been too small and fast for them.
- You can always tell when **someone is watching you**, even if you can't see them. Being watched by children, by hidden spies, by curious commoners — your sense for attention is preternatural. (You don't know *who* is watching, only that you're being watched.)

*Reasoning: The Urchin's L5 is "I have always been prey, and I learned to be impossible to catch." Urban terrain navigation is their home advantage. The reactive dodge is the signature — a once-per-short-rest "you missed me" that reflects the Urchin's survival instinct against bigger opponents. The watched sense is the paranoia-as-ability — someone who grew up being constantly at risk feels eyes they can't see. (Note: the "always advantage on initiative" ability from earlier drafts was cut to bring the L5 power level in line with other Themes — that's now a candidate for L11 or later if ever revisited.)*

### L11 — Street Network
**Expertise Die (d4)** on Sleight of Hand and Stealth checks.

- The forgotten children of every city are your eyes. In any settlement of town size or larger, you can spend **1 hour** making contact with the local street children (beggars, pickpockets, servants, runaways) and learn:
  - **Who in this city is searching for anyone** (including you, your party, or specific targets)
  - **What unusual activity has been happening recently** that authorities haven't noticed
  - **Where hidden things can be found or hidden** — unwatched alleyways, abandoned buildings, rooftop routes
  The children do not ask for payment in coin. They may ask for **food, a kindness, a small favor, or the simple acknowledgment that they exist**. Failure to treat them with dignity may cost you their trust permanently.
- Once per long rest, you can **vanish into the forgotten** — in any settlement, you can disappear completely for up to 24 hours. No one will find you by any mundane means (magical location spells still function). During this time, you are effectively invisible to urban searching, though you can still act — you're just moving through the gaps of society, places no one thinks to look.
- **Small and Underestimated:** Enemies who see you for the first time treat you as a non-threat. You have **advantage on the first attack roll** against any creature that hasn't seen you in combat before — they didn't think you were dangerous, and they were wrong.

*Reasoning: The Urchin's L11 is "the invisible are my people, and together we are everywhere." The street children network is the Urchin's unique social infrastructure — it mirrors the Criminal's underworld but from below it rather than within it. The payment note is crucial: these are children, and the Urchin's treatment of them matters ethically and mechanically. Vanishing into the forgotten is the disappear-at-will ability, more narrowly scoped than the Criminal's L17 Ghost but more reliable in cities. The "first attack advantage" codifies the "you don't think I'm a threat" instinct.*

### L17 — Shadow of the City
**Expertise Die scales to d6** on Sleight of Hand and Stealth.

- Once per long rest, you can **declare yourself forgotten**. For **45 minutes**, your existence becomes unremarkable to anyone who doesn't actively know you well — commoners look past you, guards ignore you, shopkeepers don't remember your face. You aren't invisible; people simply fail to register that you're there. The effect ends early if you take a hostile action or clearly identify yourself. Close allies and people who have known you for weeks are not affected; this is a waking sleep of stranger-memory, not magical alteration.
- You have honed a lifetime of being overlooked into an ability to **slip away when it matters**. Creatures pursuing you in urban environments have **disadvantage on Survival checks** to track you, and the DC to spot you while hidden in an urban environment is increased by **5**. You are extraordinarily hard to find in a city — but not impossible, especially for those who know what you are.
- **The Forgotten Network:** The street children you've protected and treated with dignity have become a **regional network of eyes and hands**. You always know, within hours of arriving in any city you've visited before, if there is an active threat to you — an assassin in town, a bounty posted, a warrant issued. Once per campaign arc, in any city with your network, you can call on the street children for **collective action** — they create a coordinated distraction, a swarm disappearance, a pickpocket campaign, or a web of misinformation spanning the entire city. The specific outcome depends on what's feasible, but an entire city's street children acting in concert can accomplish extraordinary things for those who earned their loyalty.
- The DM should occasionally surface the **cost** of this network: children you knew who didn't survive, street children now grown into people with their own needs, moments where the network expects *you* to show up for *them*.

*Reasoning: The Urchin's capstone is "I am everywhere and no one sees me — for a little while." Declare Yourself Forgotten is the headline — a 45-minute social invisibility once per day, narrower in duration than before but distinct from the Criminal's magical invisibility. The urban stealth passive reinforces the Urchin's identity without making them untrackable. The street children network at L17 becomes a campaign-scale organizing ability, but the cost note is essential — the children who trusted the Urchin had real lives, some didn't survive, and the network's loyalty is earned and maintained through continued ethical treatment. This prevents the Urchin from treating the network as a pure mechanical tool; it's a relationship.*
